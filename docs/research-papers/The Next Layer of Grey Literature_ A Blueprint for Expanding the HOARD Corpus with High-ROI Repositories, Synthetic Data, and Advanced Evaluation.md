# The Next Layer of Grey Literature: A Blueprint for Expanding the HOARD Corpus with High-ROI Repositories, Synthetic Data, and Advanced Evaluation

## Identifying and Integrating New Digital Repository Sources

The expansion of the HOARD fine-tuning corpus hinges on the identification and integration of new digital repositories of archaeological grey literature. The strategic objective is to secure high-volume, high-quality data under permissive licenses (CC-BY or CC0) with accessible APIs. An analysis of potential sources across Europe, Australia, and non-Western regions reveals a landscape of significant opportunity tempered by critical information gaps. The initial baseline established by major sources like the Archaeology Data Service (ADS) in the UK (>50,000 reports, CC-BY 4.0) [[51](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/235980720_A_Note_on_the_Archaeological_Report)], The Digital Archaeological Record (tDAR) in the US (Section 106 compliance reports, authenticated API) [[50](https://open.alberta.ca/dataset/bd6da82d-03ca-4ae6-b642-f779d4fbaa7a/resource/1cb6b881-54d5-4f3d-885e-8111eedc97a0/download/cmsw-archaeological-survey-information-bulletin-archaeological-report-guidelines.pdf)], and Open Context (structured CIDOC-CRM linked data) [[14](https://zenodo.org/records/14609210/files/EAC10guidance-2025march.pdf?download=1)] provides a strong foundation, but its Western-centric nature necessitates a global expansion strategy. The success of this strategy will be determined not just by data volume, but by the trifecta of quality, accessibility, and licensing generosity.

European repositories represent the most immediate and promising frontier for expansion. Several national archives and consortia have been identified as potential sources, including the Netherlands' DANS, Germany's IANUS/NFDI4Objects, France's Nakala, and Sweden's National Heritage Board. These institutions are central nodes in Europe's cultural heritage infrastructure, making them logical targets for acquiring a diverse range of archaeological reports [[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507)]. The IANUS consortium, part of the broader NFDI4Objects initiative, appears particularly relevant due to its focus on cultural heritage data and its alignment with German Denkmalpflege reporting traditions, which directly supports the project's multilingual adaptation goals [[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507), [69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. Similarly, DANS and Nakala are established national archives with extensive holdings, suggesting they contain valuable, albeit potentially unquantified, volumes of grey literature [[59](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/264466466_Digital_Preservation_Online_access_and_historical_archaeology_'grey_literature'_from_New_South_Wales_Australia), [60](https://www.academia.edu/2438712/NSW_Archaeology_On_Line_Grey_Literature_Archive)]. However, a critical gap exists in the provided source material: there is no definitive, up-to-date information confirming the status of these repositories for 2025-2026 releases, nor are there specifics on the volume of newly digitized archaeological reports, their licensing terms, or the availability of APIs. The existence of a sustainable digital archive for NSW historical archaeology is confirmed, but its focus on pre-1995 literature makes it less suitable for training a modern SLM on contemporary reporting standards [[51](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/235980720_A_Note_on_the_Archaeological_Report), [60](https://www.academia.edu/2438712/NSW_Archaeology_On_Line_Grey_Literature_Archive)]. The lack of detailed, public-facing data on these European platforms means that direct outreach and monitoring of their API endpoints will be essential next steps to move from promising leads to actionable data sources.

The Australian archaeological data landscape presents a more challenging picture for immediate corpus expansion. While state-level initiatives exist, such as NSW Archaeology On-Line (AOL), the available information suggests a lower return on investment compared to other regions [[59](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/264466466_Digital_Preservation_Online_access_and_historical_archaeology_'grey_literature'_from_New_South_Wales_Australia), [60](https://www.academia.edu/2438712/NSW_Archaeology_On_Line_Grey_Literature_Archive)]. The AOL archive was first established to provide online access to 'grey literature', and its digital preservation standards are well-documented [[59](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/264466466_Digital_Preservation_Online_access_and_historical_archaeology_'grey_literature'_from_New_South_Wales_Australia)]. However, its primary focus on pre-1995 historical archaeology limits its utility for training a model on current methodologies and terminologies [[51](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/235980720_A_Note_on_the_Archaeological_Report), [60](https://www.academia.edu/2438712/NSW_Archaeology_On_Line_Grey_Literature_Archive)]. There is no specific information available regarding the digital holdings, accessibility, or licensing of Victoria Heritage, another potential source mentioned in the initial scope. This indicates that the Australian market may not yet offer large, modern, and openly licensed datasets of archaeological reports. The resources dedicated to investigating this region might be better allocated to other areas with higher potential or to developing synthetic data generation pipelines for specialist report types.

The most strategically vital, yet data-scarce, area for expansion is the non-Western world. To achieve a globally representative model, integrating data from India, China, the Middle East, Africa, and South America is paramount. In India, the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI), established in 1861, stands out as a premier authority and a potential repository of immense value [[41](https://www.vajiraoinstitute.com/upsc-ias-current-affairs/archaeological-survey-of-india.aspx)]. Its long history and vast collection could dramatically diversify the HOARD corpus beyond its current Western focus. The ASI represents a top-tier target, though concrete details on its digital holdings, licensing, or API access are not available in the provided context. For the remaining regions—China, the Middle East, Africa, and South America—the provided materials contain no specific information on any new or existing digital repositories for archaeological grey literature. This represents a significant blind spot and a major opportunity for primary research. The current state of archaeological data sharing heavily favors European and North American sources, as evidenced by literature reviews focused on satellite remote sensing for cultural heritage protection [[90](https://www.mdpi.com/2571-9408/9/4/149)]. Addressing this imbalance is crucial for the long-term relevance and robustness of the HOARD pipeline.

To guide the selection process, a systematic evaluation framework based on Return on Investment (ROI), calculated as Volume × Quality × Permissive License, is necessary. ## Synthetic Data Generation for Rare Archaeological Report Types

A fundamental challenge in training robust language models on archaeological grey literature is the long-tail distribution of report types. While common reports detailing standard evaluations and trenching are abundant, specialist reports—such as those on geoarchaeology, osteology, archaeobotany, maritime archaeology, and building recording—are critical for comprehensive understanding but are inherently rare [[66](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359099161_Creating_Qualitative_Datasets_in_Geoarchaeology_An_easy-applicable_description_template_for_archaeological_thin_section_analysis_using_Stoops_2003), [68](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35342719/)]. Synthetic data augmentation offers a powerful method to address this scarcity, enabling the creation of realistic training examples for these underrepresented categories. The proposed techniques range from simple template-based generation to sophisticated knowledge distillation from larger cloud models, all while navigating the critical risk of generating factually incorrect information, or "hallucinations." A successful strategy requires a hybrid approach that balances automation with rigorous validation.

One of the most straightforward and reliable techniques for synthetic data generation is template-based creation, which is highly applicable to specialist reports with standardized structures. For example, geoarchaeological reports often rely on descriptive templates for analyzing thin sections of soil samples [[66](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359099161_Creating_Qualitative_Datasets_in_Geoarchaeology_An_easy-applicable_description_template_for_archaeological_thin_section_analysis_using_Stoops_2003), [68](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35342719/)]. By populating these templates with plausible but varied data points (e.g., different soil types, artifact inclusions, sedimentary structures), it is possible to generate a large volume of synthetic reports that mirror the structure and terminology of real documents. This method ensures a high degree of structural fidelity and reduces the risk of generating nonsensical content. Another related technique is data-to-text conversion, where structured data, such as a formal catalogue of mollusc taxa [[15](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/jqs.70041)] or a database of radiocarbon (C14) results, is converted into coherent prose. Leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) for this task allows for the generation of natural-sounding descriptions and interpretations based on factual inputs, bridging the gap between quantitative data and qualitative narrative.

For generating more complex and varied synthetic prose, leveraging the few-shot learning capabilities of LLMs is a highly effective strategy [[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.19580v5)]. Few-shot learning allows a model to perform a task after being given only a few examples, a capability demonstrated by models like Llama 2 in creating synthetic medical records [[67](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08664)] and in question-answering tasks [[65](https://aclanthology.org/2024.lrec-main.1153/)]. This approach involves curating a small set of high-quality, human-written examples for a rare report type and using them as prompts to guide a powerful cloud-based LLM (e.g., GPT-4) in generating a much larger synthetic dataset. Prompt engineering plays a crucial role here; by carefully crafting the initial prompt and examples, users can fine-tune the model's output style and content to align with specific requirements [[64](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/piyush-ranjan-9297a632_tackling-hallucination-in-llms-mitigation-activity-7288759751625093120-F3wR)]. This method has been successfully applied in various domains, including few-shot dialog state tracking (SynthDST) and clinical record generation, demonstrating its versatility and power [[47](https://machinelearning.apple.com/research/synthdst), [67](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08664)]. Furthermore, knowledge distillation from larger, more capable cloud models can be employed to transfer expertise to a smaller, local SLM suitable for offline deployment. This involves using the larger model to generate synthetic text, which is then used to fine-tune the smaller model, effectively compressing the knowledge into a more efficient package [[45](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.04338), [48](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/use-cases/synthetic-data-generation-for-agentic-ai/)].

The primary and most significant risk associated with synthetic data generation is the production of hallucinations—content that is fluent and syntactically correct but factually inaccurate or unsupported by any evidence [[87](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.06265v3), [91](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12518350/)]. Given that archaeological reports are built upon a foundation of empirical evidence, the generation of false facts regarding stratigraphy, chronology, or artifact typology would be catastrophic for the training process. The literature highlights this concern across multiple fields, including medicine, where accurate patient information is paramount [[67](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08664)]. Mitigating this risk requires a multi-layered defense-in-depth strategy. First, careful prompt engineering can constrain the model's output space and bias it towards factual responses [[64](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/piyush-ranjan-9297a632_tackling-hallucination-in-llms-mitigation-activity-7288759751625093120-F3wR)]. Second, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) can ground the LLM's outputs in a reliable, curated knowledge base containing authoritative data on ceramic typologies, C14 calibration curves, or geological formations [[56](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/5/856)]. Third, fine-tuning the generative model itself on fact-checked datasets has been shown to improve its inherent reliability and reduce hallucination rates [[62](https://openreview.net/forum?id=x9OPC7j6hh), [85](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-2688/6/10/260)]. Fourth, automated detection tools can be deployed to flag potentially hallucinatory content. These tools, sometimes combined with Natural Language Inference (NLI) models, can help identify inconsistencies and unsupported claims [[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.11094v2), [19](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/40254/44215)]. Finally, and most critically, a human-in-the-loop validation protocol is non-negotiable. Expert archaeologists must rigorously review and validate all synthetic data before it is added to the training corpus [[39](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ai-archaeology-uncovering-layers-past-precision-insight-richard-ott-mo8le)]. Their professional judgment is the ultimate safeguard against propagating errors and ensuring the integrity of the HOARD pipeline.

In practice, a hybrid synthetic data pipeline offers the best path forward. This pipeline would begin with template-based generation for the most structured and formulaic report types, such as geoarchaeological thin section analyses [[66](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359099161_Creating_Qualitative_Datasets_in_Geoarchaeology_An_easy-applicable_description_template_for_archaeological_thin_section_analysis_using_Stoops_2003)]. For less structured but still niche report types, a few-shot prompting method would be employed. This would involve starting with a small, meticulously curated seed set of real-world examples provided by domain experts. These seeds would guide a powerful, cloud-based LLM in generating a larger pool of synthetic drafts. Each draft would then undergo mandatory review by an expert archaeologist to verify factual accuracy, logical coherence, and stylistic appropriateness. Only data that passes this rigorous human validation would be incorporated into the final fine-tuning dataset. This hybrid approach strikes a balance between the need for a large volume of data to properly train the SLM and the absolute necessity of maintaining the highest standards of factual accuracy, thereby mitigating the core risks of synthetic data generation.

## Multilingual and Multi-Jurisdiction Adaptation Strategies

To transcend its current focus on UK and US English, the HOARD pipeline must develop robust strategies for multilingual and multi-jurisdiction adaptation. The archaeological reporting traditions of countries like France, Germany, Spain/Portugal, and Japan possess unique vocabularies, grammatical structures, and regulatory frameworks that cannot be adequately handled by a monolingual model [[35](https://www.apple.com/os/pdf/All_New_Features_iOS_26_Sept_2025.pdf), [69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. Successfully adapting the model to these contexts requires not only adding new linguistic capabilities but also preserving the original model's performance—a challenge known as catastrophic forgetting. Fortunately, recent advances in Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) offer sophisticated solutions to this problem, enabling modular and stable model adaptation without the need for complete retraining.

The landscape of non-English archaeological reporting is rich and varied. In France, reports from the Institut National de Recherches Archéologiques Préventives (INRAP) follow a distinct format [[69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. In Germany, the concept of *Denkmalpflege* (monument care) dictates specific reporting standards [[69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. Similar jurisdiction-specific conventions exist in Spain and Portugal, as well as in Japan, where the term 埋蔵文化財 (*bunkazai*) refers to buried cultural properties and governs a specific reporting pathway [[69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. These differences extend beyond vocabulary to encompass entire regulatory ecosystems. For instance, a report compliant with the UK's Chartered Institute for Archaeologists (CIfA) regulations, specified under the MoRPHE guidelines, will have a different structure and emphasis than a US report prepared under Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act [[40](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17567505.2021.1963573), [69](https://www.academia.edu/44032671/The_Industrial_Archaeology_of_the_Archaeology_Industry)]. Adapting the HOARD model to understand and generate text within these diverse legal and linguistic contexts is a core requirement for its global applicability. Current small language models may struggle with this specialized multilingual vocabulary, making targeted adaptation essential [[9](https://aclanthology.org/2025.naacl-long.570/)].

The primary technical hurdle in this adaptation process is catastrophic forgetting. When a neural network, including a language model, is fine-tuned on a new task (e.g., translating from English to French), its performance on the original task (e.g., English text generation) can degrade sharply [[3](https://soapubs.com/index.php/AIDT/article/download/1380/1258), [83](https://openreview.net/forum?id=VrHiF2hsrm)]. This phenomenon occurs because the learned weights of the model are overwritten during the new training phase, erasing previously acquired knowledge [[82](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19546)]. For a system like HOARD, which relies on a well-trained base model, catastrophic forgetting would be unacceptable. The solution lies in Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, which aim to adapt a model with minimal changes to its parameters. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is a prominent example of PEFT, where trainable low-rank matrices are inserted into the layers of a pre-trained transformer model, leaving the original weights frozen [[1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.09684), [33](https://www.atlantis-press.com/proceedings/iwadic-25/126023322)]. This makes fine-tuning computationally efficient and reduces the risk of forgetting, as the majority of the model's knowledge remains intact [[34](https://www.legionintel.com/blog/navigating-the-challenges-of-fine-tuning-and-catastrophic-forgetting)].

However, standard LoRA is not a complete solution to catastrophic forgetting. Research shows that learned updates can still interfere with the original model's capabilities [[2](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/40703), [6](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/402626824_OPLoRA_Orthogonal_Projection_LoRA_Prevents_Catastrophic_Forgetting_During_Parameter-Efficient_Fine-Tuning), [78](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/40703/44664)]. Consequently, the field has developed more advanced PEFT methods specifically designed to mitigate this issue. One of the most promising is Orthogonal Projection LoRA (OPLoRA). This technique constrains the optimization of the new LoRA weights to an orthogonal subspace relative to the original model's weight gradients, effectively preventing interference and preserving the pre-trained knowledge [[5](https://ideas.repec.org/a/axf/aidtaa/v3y2026i1p52-61.html), [44](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.13003)]. Studies have demonstrated that OPLoRA can effectively alleviate catastrophic forgetting during continual instruction fine-tuning [[5](https://ideas.repec.org/a/axf/aidtaa/v3y2026i1p52-61.html)]. Other innovative PEFT methods include LoRA without Forgetting (LoRAF), which uses freezing and sparse masking to further reduce trainable parameters and mitigate forgetting [[70](https://openreview.net/forum?id=aGOQYJfz6H&referrer=%5Bthe%20profile%20of%20Jiacheng%20You%5D(%2Fprofile%3Fid%3D~Jiacheng_You1))]. An alternative architectural approach involves creating a unified multilingual model by adding a new language-specific output head rather than overwriting the existing one. This method has been proven to be more effective at mitigating performance degradation on source tasks compared to traditional fine-tuning [[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.04844v1), [77](https://openreview.net/forum?id=hESsatbRee)]. For the HOARD project, adopting a PEFT strategy is not merely an efficiency gain but a necessity for maintaining model stability. The evidence strongly suggests that OPLoRA is the most direct and effective solution to the specific problem of catastrophic forgetting identified in the literature [[5](https://ideas.repec.org/a/axf/aidtaa/v3y2026i1p52-61.html), [44](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.13003)].

The recommended implementation strategy is to build a modular, PEFT-driven architecture. Instead of fine-tuning the entire base model for each new language or jurisdiction, separate LoRA modules would be created. Each module would be trained on data specific to a target context, such as French INRAP reports or German Denkmalpflege documentation. During inference, the appropriate LoRA module could be activated alongside the frozen base model, allowing the system to switch between different linguistic and regulatory contexts without any permanent modification to the core model weights. This modularity preserves the original capabilities of the HOARD model while providing flexible and scalable adaptation. Before full-scale deployment, a proof-of-concept study comparing the performance of standard LoRA against the more advanced OPLoRA would be highly advisable to quantify the reduction in catastrophic forgetting and confirm its effectiveness for the specific task of archaeological report generation. This approach ensures that as the HOARD pipeline expands its linguistic and geographic reach, it does so without compromising the quality and reliability of its foundational capabilities.

## Domain-Specific Evaluation Frameworks for Archaeological Reports

Assessing the quality of generated archaeological reports requires moving beyond generic Natural Language Processing (NLP) metrics like BLEU and ROUGE, which measure n-gram overlap and fail to capture the nuanced requirements of archaeological interpretation [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396668725_Digital_Stratigraphy-A_Pattern_Analysis_Framework_Integrating_Computer_Forensics_Criminology_and_Forensic_Archaeology_for_Crime_Scene_Investigation), [24](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-6756/5/4/48)]. The true quality of a report lies in its logical consistency, evidentiary fidelity, and adherence to regulatory standards. Therefore, developing a domain-specific evaluation framework is a critical component of enhancing the HOARD pipeline. This framework must integrate novel automated metrics that probe the semantic and structural integrity of the generated text with a rigorous human evaluation protocol grounded in professional archaeological practice. This dual-arm approach ensures both scalability and the retention of expert oversight.

A cornerstone of archaeological reasoning is stratigraphic logic, which seeks to reconstruct the sequence of deposition and formation processes at a site. The Harris Matrix is the canonical tool for representing these relationships [[61](https://www.scribd.com/document/360997411/AnAlyticAl-ArchAeology-and-ArtificiAl-AdAptive-SyStemS)]. An automated evaluation metric can be developed to assess the logical consistency of a generated report's stratigraphic narrative. This would involve extracting statements about temporal and spatial relationships from the text and attempting to construct a matrix. The resulting matrix could then be scored based on its internal consistency—for example, by checking for cycles (which would indicate a logical error) and assessing how well the inferred sequence aligns with the input data or a reference matrix. The concept of Stratigraphic Reconstruction Consistency (SRC) has already been proposed and measured in related research, serving as a valuable precedent [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396668725_Digital_Stratigraphy-A_Pattern_Analysis_Framework_Integrating_Computer_Forensics_Criminology_and_Forensic_Archaeology_for_Crime_Scene_Investigation), [24](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-6756/5/4/48)]. By applying and refining such a metric, the HOARD pipeline can receive quantitative feedback on its ability to generate logically sound stratigraphic narratives, a far more meaningful measure of quality than simple lexical similarity.

Equally important is the fidelity of the report's evidence base, particularly its finds catalogue. An archaeological report is an argument built upon a foundation of physical evidence. Errors in citing ceramic typologies, assigning incorrect dates to coins, or misrepresenting radiocarbon results render a report unreliable [[27](https://www.tiktok.com/@itsaihistory/video/7588557906434329870)]. An evaluation metric for finds catalogue fidelity would compare the information presented in the generated text against a structured, machine-readable source, such as a CSV file or a database of recorded artifacts [[15](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/jqs.70041)]. This would involve named entity recognition to identify references to specific artifacts, followed by a verification step to check if the associated attributes (e.g., type, date, material) are accurately described. This metric directly addresses the risk of factual hallucinations and ensures that the model is correctly processing and presenting evidence. The evaluation of regulatory compliance is another critical dimension. Reports from different jurisdictions, such as those required under Section 106 in the US or by Scotland's Coastal Heritage at Risk Project, must adhere to specific template structures and content requirements [[40](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17567505.2021.1963573), [50](https://open.alberta.ca/dataset/bd6da82d-03ca-4ae6-b642-f779d4fbaa7a/resource/1cb6b881-54d5-4f3d-885e-8111eedc97a0/download/cmsw-archaeological-survey-information-bulletin-archaeological-report-guidelines.pdf)]. An automated checker can be programmed to scan the generated text for the presence and correct formatting of mandated sections (e.g., Executive Summary, Methodology, Conclusion, GIS Data Appendix). This ensures that the output is not only semantically correct but also practically useful and compliant with legal mandates.

While automated metrics provide essential scalability, they cannot replace the nuanced judgment of a human expert. AI-driven evaluation cannot substitute for human baselines in foundation model assessments [[81](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13776), [95](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=VbG9sIsn4F)]. A robust human evaluation protocol is therefore indispensable for validating the automated systems and capturing aspects of quality that algorithms cannot. This protocol should be built around a detailed scoring rubric, similar to those used in educational technology to evaluate AI-generated interfaces [[89](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.10773v1)]. Drawing inspiration from clinician-informed checklists for explainable AI, a task force of volunteer archaeologists could develop a 14-item checklist covering clinical, machine, and decision attributes [[94](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12167368/)]. This rubric would require human evaluators to score generated reports across several key dimensions: factual correctness (verifying key claims against source data), logical consistency (assessing the coherence of the argument and stratigraphic narrative), clarity of writing, and adherence to the required regulatory format [[89](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.10773v1)]. To ensure reliability and transparency, multiple evaluators should score the same reports, and inter-rater reliability should be calculated [[93](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.13776v1)]. The results from this human evaluation process would serve as the "ground truth," allowing researchers to calibrate the automated metrics and establish a gold standard for HOARD's performance. This hybrid framework, combining the efficiency of automated checks with the irreplaceable insight of expert human review, provides the most comprehensive and trustworthy method for evaluating the quality of archaeological reports generated by the HOARD pipeline.

## Integrated Strategy for Corpus Expansion and Model Enhancement

To achieve its research goal, the HOARD project must adopt an integrated strategy that holistically combines corpus expansion, synthetic data augmentation, multilingual adaptation, and domain-specific evaluation. This approach moves beyond isolated improvements to create a synergistic system where each component reinforces the others, leading to a more robust, versatile, and globally relevant fine-tuning pipeline. The overarching objective is to evolve HOARD from a tool trained primarily on UK and US grey literature into a comprehensive platform capable of handling the diverse linguistic, regulatory, and technical demands of global archaeological practice. The following synthesized recommendations outline a phased, risk-managed plan to realize this vision.

First, the strategy must prioritize high-Return on Investment (ROI) corpus expansion. ROI, defined as Volume multiplied by Quality multiplied by Permissive License, should guide all acquisition efforts. The single most impactful action is to pursue data from the **Archaeological Survey of India (ASI)**. As a premier national authority with a vast collection dating back to 1861, the ASI promises to dramatically increase the corpus's volume and, more importantly, its global diversity, breaking the Western-centric bias of current datasets [[41](https://www.vajiraoinstitute.com/upsc-ias-current-affairs/archaeological-survey-of-india.aspx)]. Immediate priority should be given to initiating direct contact with the ASI to ascertain the volume, licensing terms, and API accessibility of its digital holdings. Concurrently, a targeted outreach campaign should be launched to investigate the suitability of European repositories, particularly **IANUS/NFDI4Objects**, which aligns perfectly with the project's multilingual goals [[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507)]. For other non-Western regions where data is scarce, a systematic literature and institutional review is necessary to identify potential partners. This two-pronged approach—pursuing high-value existing data while systematically exploring new frontiers—forms the bedrock of the corpus expansion strategy.

Second, to address the chronic scarcity of specialist report types, a **hybrid synthetic data pipeline** should be implemented. This strategy balances automation with rigorous validation to produce high-quality training data without compromising factual accuracy. Phase one will focus on low-risk, high-structure data types like geoarchaeological thin section descriptions, using template-based generation guided by established description templates to ensure structural and terminological fidelity [[66](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359099161_Creating_Qualitative_Datasets_in_Geoarchaeology_An_easy-applicable_description_template_for_archaeological_thin_section_analysis_using_Stoops_2003), [68](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35342719/)]. Phase two will tackle more complex report types using a few-shot prompting technique. This involves using a small, expert-curated seed set of real examples to guide a powerful cloud-based LLM in generating a larger volume of synthetic text [[65](https://aclanthology.org/2024.lrec-main.1153/), [67](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08664)]. Critically, every piece of synthetic data produced through this process must undergo mandatory review and validation by a domain expert archaeologist to detect and eliminate hallucinations and factual errors [[39](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ai-archaeology-uncovering-layers-past-precision-insight-richard-ott-mo8le), [87](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.06265v3)]. This hybrid approach ensures that the model is exposed to a wider variety of specialist content, improving its ability to interpret and generate nuanced, evidence-based reports.

Third, to enable multilingual and multi-jurisdictional operation, the project must implement a **parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) strategy** to prevent catastrophic forgetting. Rather than overwriting the base model's weights, this approach involves creating modular, language-specific LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) adapters [[1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.09684)]. For each new language or jurisdiction (e.g., French INRAP, German Denkmalpflege), a separate adapter will be trained on a specialized dataset. During use, the appropriate adapter is loaded with the frozen base model, allowing the system to switch contexts seamlessly. Among the available PEFT methods, **OPLoRA (Orthogonal Projection LoRA)** is the most suitable candidate, as research indicates it is specifically designed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting by constraining updates to orthogonal subspaces, thus preserving the original model's knowledge [[5](https://ideas.repec.org/a/axf/aidtaa/v3y2026i1p52-61.html), [44](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.13003)]. Adopting this modular, PEFT-driven architecture is crucial for maintaining the stability and reliability of the core model while enabling scalable and flexible adaptation to new linguistic and regulatory environments.

Finally, the entire enhancement cycle must be governed by a **dual-arm evaluation framework**. This framework integrates automated metrics for scalability with a rigorous human evaluation protocol for quality assurance. Automated metrics will be developed to quantify key aspects of report quality, including **Stratigraphic Reconstruction Consistency (SRC)** to evaluate logical sequencing and **Regulatory Compliance Checks** to ensure adherence to jurisdiction-specific templates [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396668725_Digital_Stratigraphy-A_Pattern_Analysis_Framework_Integrating_Computer_Forensics_Criminology_and_Forensic_Archaeology_for_Crime_Scene_Investigation), [50](https://open.alberta.ca/dataset/bd6da82d-03ca-4ae6-b642-f779d4fbaa7a/resource/1cb6b881-54d5-4f3d-885e-8111eedc97a0/download/cmsw-archaeological-survey-information-bulletin-archaeological-report-guidelines.pdf)]. These metrics will provide continuous, quantitative feedback on model performance. Simultaneously, a team of volunteer archaeologists should be assembled to develop and apply a detailed, expert-driven scoring rubric. This human evaluation, based on principles of rigorous and transparent assessment, will serve as the gold standard for validating the automated systems and capturing qualitative aspects of report quality that algorithms cannot [[81](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13776), [95](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=VbG9sIsn4F)]. The results from both arms of the evaluation will be correlated to refine the automated metrics and establish a definitive benchmark for the HOARD pipeline's performance. By pursuing this interconnected and methodologically sound strategy, the HOARD project can systematically enhance its capabilities, paving the way for a new generation of AI tools that support and elevate the practice of archaeological science worldwide.
