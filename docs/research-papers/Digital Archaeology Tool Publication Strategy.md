# **Strategic Deployment and Publication Pathways for HOARD: Integrating Local AI Models into Archaeological Workflows**

The landscape of digital archaeology is currently undergoing a profound and necessary transformation, characterized by a rapid shift from closed, proprietary software ecosystems to transparent, open-source methodologies and reproducible digital workflows.1 Within this evolving paradigm, the commercial archaeology and cultural resource management (CRM) sectors face a critical, persistent operational bottleneck: the timely synthesis of massive quantities of raw field data into standardized, compliant grey literature reports. Grey literature—the unpublished technical reports detailing the results of development-led excavations—constitutes the vast majority of primary archaeological knowledge generated today.3 However, the manual drafting of these reports represents a significant drain on time and resources for heritage organizations.  
HOARD, an open-source, MIT-licensed Python command-line interface (CLI) tool utilizing local artificial intelligence models, directly addresses this sector-wide workflow challenge. By running fully offline, HOARD guarantees data sovereignty—a critical requirement for CRM firms handling sensitive, pre-development client data—ensuring that no proprietary or embargoed information leaves the host machine via cloud API calls. To transition HOARD from a functional, validated technical pipeline into a widely adopted, scientifically credible, and formally citable tool within both the commercial and academic heritage sectors, a highly sophisticated deployment strategy is required.  
This comprehensive research report provides an exhaustive analysis of the publication venues, software validation frameworks, preprint strategies, community dissemination channels, and potential funding and pilot partnerships necessary to embed HOARD deeply within the archaeological community.

## **The Academic Publication Landscape for Digital Archaeology Software**

The formal publication of software tools in archaeological and heritage science journals serves two foundational functions. First, it confers academic legitimacy and intellectual credibility through the process of rigorous peer review. Second, it provides a stable Digital Object Identifier (DOI), which facilitates citation tracking, allowing developers to receive formal scholarly credit for their software engineering efforts.4 The selection of an appropriate target journal for HOARD must carefully balance audience reach (particularly the divide between commercial practitioners and academic theoreticians), formatting constraints, editorial processing times, and the financial implications of Article Processing Charges (APCs).

### **In-Depth Journal Profiles and Suitability Analysis**

The quantitative metrics provided in the table above must be contextualized within the specific editorial philosophies and target demographics of each publication. The following analysis evaluates the specific suitability of each venue for HOARD.

#### **1\. Journal of Computer Applications in Archaeology (JCAA)**

The *Journal of Computer Applications in Archaeology* (JCAA) serves as the official publication of the Computer Applications and Quantitative Methods in Archaeology (CAA) international community.19 It is a premier venue for computational archaeology and explicitly accepts software tool papers and system architecture papers, particularly those designed for data management, spatial infrastructure, and automated reporting.20

* **Editorial Workflow and Constraints:** JCAA has instituted a highly specific, progressive submission pipeline designed to foster open science. Manuscripts cannot be submitted directly in the traditional sense; they must first be submitted as preprints to a repository (such as Zenodo) to obtain a DOI, and then submitted to *Peer Community In (PCI) Archaeology* for open peer review.5 Once the paper is publicly recommended (peer-reviewed) by PCI, the authors are granted the opportunity to publish the finalized article in JCAA upon payment of the £300 APC, or alternatively, they may publish it in the CAA Proceedings entirely free of charge.22  
* **Suitability for HOARD (High):** The 5,000-word limit imposed by JCAA is ideal for comprehensively explaining HOARD's CLI architecture, the intricacies of its underlying local AI models, and its transformative impact on CRM grey literature workflows.22 However, the strict requirement to utilize the PCI Archaeology pipeline necessitates a robust, early-stage preprint strategy, which will extend the timeline from initial drafting to final journal publication.

#### **2\. Internet Archaeology**

Established as one of the oldest and most prestigious open-access digital archaeology journals, *Internet Archaeology* is deeply embedded within the UK CRM, heritage management, and academic sectors.8

* **Format and Focus:** The journal’s editorial policy explicitly imposes no arbitrary word limits and actively encourages the publication of data, source code, visualisations, and interactive digital methodologies.8 It possesses a strong, documented history of publishing foundational papers on open-source software engineering, spatial data infrastructure, and the sociotechnical practices of digital archaeology.24  
* **Suitability for HOARD (Very High):** Because *Internet Archaeology* explicitly bridges the persistent gap between academic research and "development-led" (commercial) archaeology, it perfectly aligns with HOARD's intended niche professional audience.8 The primary barrier to entry is financial; the variable APC structure, which scales with article complexity and length (e.g., approximately £1,400 for a standard 5,000-word article, or £500 for a shorter data paper), requires the developer to secure external funding prior to acceptance.9 Nevertheless, its prestige and direct readership among heritage professionals ensure maximum visibility and immediate practical credibility.

#### **3\. Journal of Open Archaeology Data (JOAD)**

While JOAD was primarily designed as a venue for high-reuse archaeological datasets, it increasingly serves as an efficient publication mechanism for software, computational tools, and code-based methodologies that interact with archaeological data.26

* **Format and Focus:** JOAD utilizes highly structured, concise paper templates. These templates are not designed for expansive theoretical narratives; rather, they act as rich, peer-reviewed metadata documents describing the circumstances of data collection or the functionality of a software tool.12 The software or data must be permanently deposited in an open repository prior to submission.12  
* **Suitability for HOARD (Moderate to High):** If the primary goal is a rapid, low-cost (£100 APC) publication to secure a citable DOI and establish priority of invention, JOAD is an excellent, frictionless option.11 However, if the intention for HOARD is to provide a detailed, long-form narrative outlining the theoretical and ethical implications of deploying AI in the generation of grey literature, the restrictive templates of JOAD will prove insufficient.

#### **4\. Heritage Science (Springer Nature)**

*Heritage Science* is a high-impact journal that publishes original research on scientific, mathematical, and computational methods applied to cultural heritage objects, materials, and landscapes.28

* **Suitability for HOARD (Low to Moderate):** This journal leans heavily toward the hard sciences—materials science, complex 3D photogrammetric modeling, chemical conservation algorithms, and microscopic imaging.29 While it does accept computational papers, a Python CLI tool focused on text generation, data parsing, and workflow optimization for grey literature reports may struggle to meet the journal's specific scientific scope. Unless the manuscript heavily emphasizes a novel mathematical or algorithmic breakthrough in how the local AI model processes archaeological text, *Heritage Science* is likely a misalignment of audience and scope. Furthermore, its high APC (£1,490 / $1,890) presents a significant financial hurdle for independent, open-source developers.17

#### **5\. Archaeological Prospection (John Wiley & Sons)**

* **Suitability for HOARD (Low):** This journal is strictly focused on shallow near-surface geophysics, remote sensing (e.g., LiDAR, photogrammetry), and geospatial visualization.31 Unless HOARD processes magnetometry, Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR), or raw spatial coordinates to generate its reports, it falls completely outside the editorial scope of this publication.

#### **6\. Journal of Open Source Software (JOSS)**

JOSS represents a paradigm shift in academic publishing, operating entirely outside traditional editorial management systems.4 It is highly suited for software developers, but it carries highly specific eligibility criteria that require a dedicated analysis, which is provided in the subsequent section of this report.

## **The Journal of Open Source Software (JOSS): Eligibility, Process, and Vulnerabilities**

The *Journal of Open Source Software* (JOSS) offers a highly attractive, developer-centric route for HOARD. It features no Article Processing Charges (APC), utilizes a lightweight Markdown format, and operates on a rapid, transparent review cycle.14 Because HOARD is a Python CLI tool, is pip-installable, is released under an OSI-approved MIT license, features Continuous Integration (CI) testing, and is well-documented, it mathematically fulfills the baseline technical prerequisites for JOSS submission.37 However, a nuanced evaluation of HOARD against JOSS’s recent policy updates reveals significant strategic vulnerabilities that must be mitigated prior to submission.

### **The JOSS Submission Process for a CLI AI Pipeline**

The JOSS process abandons traditional editorial workflows in favor of a collaborative, GitHub-based system.4 The process unfolds as follows:

1. **Preparation:** The author writes a brief paper.md file located in the root of the software repository. This document focuses almost entirely on a "Statement of Need," detailing the problem the software solves, the target audience, and a comparison to existing tools.37 It does not report new archaeological findings.  
2. **Submission:** The author submits via a brief web form, which triggers a JOSS bot to open a pre-review issue on the openjournals/joss-reviews GitHub repository.14  
3. **Conversational Peer Review:** An editor assigns two or more reviewers who clone the repository, install the software, run the tests, and interact conversationally with the author via GitHub issue comments.14 The author is expected to push code commits and documentation updates to their repository in real-time to address reviewer feedback.14  
4. **Archiving and Publication:** Upon successful review, the author must mint a permanent archive DOI (typically via Zenodo) for the accepted version of the software. JOSS then assigns a paper DOI and formally publishes the article.14

### **Anticipated Reviewer Flags and Strategic Vulnerabilities**

If HOARD is submitted to JOSS in its current trajectory, reviewers will apply a strict evaluation rubric. Three critical areas will likely be flagged as weaknesses, potentially leading to a desk rejection if not proactively addressed.

#### **1\. The "Research Software" vs. "Commercial Utility" Divide**

JOSS explicitly restricts its scope to *research software*—tools that solve complex modeling problems, extract knowledge from large datasets, or execute research experiments.14 The guidelines explicitly state that "minor utility" packages, thin API clients, and single-function administrative scripts are entirely ineligible for publication.14

* **The Vulnerability:** HOARD is explicitly designed for a "niche professional audience (commercial archaeologists, CRM firms, heritage consultants)" to solve a "workflow problem" related to grey literature reporting. Reviewers evaluating the tool may hastily classify it as administrative or commercial compliance software rather than genuine *research software*.  
* **The Mitigation Strategy:** The paper.md narrative must aggressively reframe the concept of "grey literature." It must be argued that grey literature is not merely a commercial compliance requirement, but rather the foundational *primary research data* of the archaeological discipline.3 HOARD must be presented as a sophisticated tool that structures, parses, and standardizes vast quantities of unstructured field data, thereby facilitating large-scale archaeological synthesis, data mining, and subsequent academic research. The focus must be on its capacity to "extract knowledge from large datasets."

#### **2\. The Strict Six-Month Public Development Rule**

To prevent developers from dumping proprietary, closed-source codebases or rapidly generated AI scripts into the public domain purely to secure a quick publication, JOSS has instituted a strict pre-review screening gate.14

* **The Vulnerability:** Projects developed privately are strictly ineligible until they possess a public record of open development spanning *at least six months prior to submission*.14 A repository made public immediately before submission, or one showing development concentrated into a brief burst of commits, will be summarily rejected.14  
* **The Mitigation Strategy:** If HOARD is currently being built in a private repository, it must be transitioned to a public GitHub repository immediately. The repository must demonstrate a genuine, organic history of tagged releases, public issue tracking, pull requests, and iterative refinement over a minimum half-year period before the JOSS submission form is completed.37

#### **3\. The 2026 Generative AI Usage Policies**

Given that HOARD utilizes local AI models to process data, and because the developer may have utilized generative AI (e.g., GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT) to assist in writing the Python codebase, the submission falls directly under JOSS's newly updated generative AI policies, instituted to combat the zero-marginal-cost generation of code.14

* **The Vulnerability:** Since 2026, JOSS requires an explicit "AI usage disclosure" statement. While the use of AI to write software, scaffold tests, or draft documentation is fully permitted, authors must clearly document which models were used, version numbers, and the specific scope of assistance.14 Most importantly, because AI significantly lowers the barrier to generating code, JOSS reviewers now heavily scrutinize the human element: the "design thinking," conceptual frameworks, and architectural choices embedded in the software.14  
* **The Mitigation Strategy:** The disclosure statement must be transparent and comprehensive. Furthermore, the paper.md must clearly articulate the specific archaeological domain expertise embedded within HOARD. Reviewers will demand to see why off-the-shelf AI wrappers are insufficient, and how HOARD's specific CLI pipeline implements deep archaeological domain knowledge to handle local, offline model orchestration safely and accurately. The emphasis must be on the human architectural decisions that govern the AI, not just the volume of code produced.

## **Community Channels, Professional Networks, and Ecosystem Integration**

While academic publication provides a DOI and essential scholarly legitimacy, it rarely acts as the primary driver for grassroots adoption among field archaeologists and commercial heritage units. To achieve widespread community adoption and disrupt established workflows, HOARD must penetrate the professional and informal digital networks where heritage consultants actively discover and evaluate new tools.39

### **Primary Professional Communities and Discovery Channels**

1. **BAJR (British Archaeological Jobs Resource):** Despite its UK-centric origins, BAJR functions as a central nervous system for commercial archaeology on an international scale. Its forums, Facebook groups, and tool-sharing threads serve as the primary digital watercoolers for field directors, post-excavation specialists, and field technicians. Promoting HOARD within the BAJR ecosystem will yield immediate, practical feedback from end-users who experience the grey literature bottleneck daily.  
2. **CIfA (Chartered Institute for Archaeologists):** Engaging with CIfA is critical for top-down, corporate adoption. CIfA sets the professional standards and guidelines for archaeological reporting and grey literature generation. Engaging with CIfA’s Information Management Special Interest Group (IMSIG) or presenting at their annual conference can position HOARD not merely as a novelty, but as a compliance tool that enhances adherence to CIfA standards, thereby driving adoption by CRM firm directors.  
3. **Aggregators and Digital Archives (ADS & Heritage Gateway):** The Archaeology Data Service (ADS) manages the mandatory digital archives for UK archaeology, while systems like Heritage Gateway aggregate historic environment records.40 While ADS does not host executable software tools for download, demonstrating that HOARD's output formats integrate seamlessly with ADS deposition standards (such as the mandatory OASIS forms) is a massive selling point. If a CRM firm knows that a report generated by HOARD is already formatted for ADS compliance, the barrier to adoption drops significantly.  
4. **Social Media and Niche Networks:**  
   * **LinkedIn:** The primary channel for reaching CRM executives, heritage NGO directors, and decision-makers who control software procurement budgets. Content here should focus on efficiency, cost-saving, and data security (the offline nature of HOARD).  
   * **Subreddits:** Communities like r/archaeology and r/gis are valuable for reaching students, early-career researchers, and tech-savvy field technicians who are likely to experiment with open-source tools and champion them upward within their organizations.

### **Open-Source Archaeology Directories**

To establish credibility within the computational sub-discipline, HOARD must be integrated into existing open-source ecosystems. It is imperative to list HOARD on **open-archaeo** (open-archaeo.info), the definitive, curated directory of open-source archaeological software and resources maintained by Zack Batist and Joe Roe.41 Submitting a pull request to the open-archaeo GitHub repository to categorize HOARD under "Data Management" or "Writing" is a mandatory step for algorithmic discoverability.43 Additionally, referencing the tool in Ben Marwick’s CRAN Task View for Archaeology on GitHub (if the data processing aspects are broadly applicable) will further embed HOARD within the digital archaeology landscape.42

## **Ecosystem Development: Pilot Partnerships and Funding Mechanisms**

Transitioning HOARD from a functional open-source prototype into an industry-standard workflow tool requires institutional validation. Identifying the right pilot partners ensures that the tool is rigorously stress-tested against real-world, high-volume, unstructured field data in secure, offline environments.

### **Identifying Ideal Pilot Partners**

The ideal pilot partner is a mid-to-large-scale CRM firm or a heavily funded academic excavation project that generates massive amounts of daily trench data and requires rapid synthesis.

* **Commercial CRM Firms:** Organizations such as **Wessex Archaeology** and **Oxford Archaeology** in the UK are uniquely positioned to act as robust pilot partners. Oxford Archaeology has a well-documented history of pioneering and adopting open-source solutions out of operational necessity, having famously developed the gvSIG OADE (Oxford Archaeology Edition) GIS fork for field documentation.51 Wessex Archaeology features a dedicated Studio team focused on digital heritage interpretation and frequently tests emerging technologies.52 Crucially, both firms prioritize client data security; HOARD's strictly offline, local-model architecture is a highly attractive proposition for handling sensitive, pre-development archaeological data without risking cloud exposure.  
* **Academic and NGO Entities:** University departments managing long-term field schools (e.g., Cornell's CIAMS or UCL's Institute of Archaeology) provide excellent, lower-risk testbeds. Additionally, major institutional entities like the **Deutsches Archäologisches Institut (DAI)**, which actively develops and maintains open-source field recording applications like *idai-field* on GitHub 53, possess the internal technical literacy to pilot, debug, and integrate HOARD into broader digital recording ecosystems.

### **Grant Programs for Heritage Technology**

Securing dedicated funding allows for sustained software maintenance, user experience (UX) design, and the computational resources required for cloud-free model fine-tuning.

* **AIA Steinmetz Endowment:** The Archaeological Institute of America (AIA) offers the Ellen and Charles Steinmetz Endowment for Archaeology. This grant provides up to $8,500 specifically to "support the use of technology in archaeological research by providing grants to archaeological projects that make innovative use of technological tools and methods".54 The deadline is routinely November 1st (e.g., Nov 1, 2026).54 This is arguably the most directly applicable grant for a solo developer building an open-source tool.  
* **AHRC (Arts and Humanities Research Council):** In the UK, the AHRC heavily funds digital infrastructure through programs like iDAH (Infrastructure for Digital Arts and Humanities) and the newly formed RICHeS (Research Infrastructure for Conservation and Heritage Science).40 Grants focused on capacity building and research software engineering are well-suited for expanding HOARD's capabilities.  
* **Heritage Lottery Fund (HLF) / Culture Recovery Fund:** These UK funds prioritize public engagement, digital accessibility, and capacity building within heritage organizations.52 A joint application submitted in partnership with a CRM firm to "modernize, accelerate, and secure digital reporting pipelines" using HOARD could be highly competitive.

## **Preprint Strategy and Open Science Workflows**

The open science movement has rapidly normalized the use of preprints within archaeology to circumvent the notoriously long delays of traditional peer review and to ensure immediate, open access to methodological advancements.56 A robust preprint strategy is essential for establishing priority and generating early community feedback for HOARD.

* **Standard Practices and Platforms:** The digital archaeology community heavily relies on **SocArXiv** and **Zenodo** for preprints.56 While *EarthArXiv* serves the geosciences effectively, it is generally considered too heavily focused on physical geography and climate science to be the optimal home for strictly archaeological software.56 Similarly, arXiv's cs.AI (Artificial Intelligence) or cs.CY (Computers and Society) categories are appropriate only if the paper is heavily focused on the mathematical or algorithmic optimization of the AI models themselves, but they lack the targeted visibility of archaeologically specific repositories.  
* **The Zenodo Advantage:** For open-source software, **Zenodo** stands as the absolute gold standard and the optimal choice for HOARD. Zenodo offers seamless, automated integration with GitHub repositories.41 Minting a DOI for a specific, tagged software release on Zenodo is a standard prerequisite for journals like JOSS.14  
* **PCI Archaeology Integration:** If JCAA is selected as the target journal, utilizing a preprint is not merely a strategic choice; it is a mandatory administrative requirement. Authors must submit their preprint to a repository (Zenodo is explicitly recommended by the CAA 5), obtain a DOI, and submit that link directly to the *Peer Community In (PCI) Archaeology* for open, transparent peer review.5

## **Strategic Synthesis and Implementation Roadmap**

To achieve both rigorous academic credibility and robust, sector-wide community adoption, the deployment of HOARD should be executed via a phased, chronological roadmap spanning the next 12 to 24 months.  
**Phase 1: Open Development and Pre-Publication (Months 1–6)** HOARD must be immediately transitioned into a public GitHub repository to initiate the required six-month open development clock mandated by JOSS and other open-source evaluators.14 During this phase, the tool should be formally registered on the open-archaeo.info directory to attract early algorithmic scrutiny and beta-testing from computational archaeologists.43 Outreach to the digital strategy teams at major CRM firms (e.g., Wessex or Oxford Archaeology) should commence, offering HOARD as an offline, zero-data-leakage solution to their reporting bottlenecks.  
**Phase 2: Preprint Deposition and Peer Review (Months 6–9)** A comprehensive software paper detailing HOARD’s architecture, its integration of local AI models, and its preservation of data sovereignty must be drafted. This manuscript should be deposited in Zenodo to secure an initial DOI. Depending on the chosen academic route, the authors should either initiate the PCI Archaeology pipeline (aiming for publication in JCAA) 5 or submit the repository directly to JOSS. If pursuing JOSS, the narrative must meticulously frame grey literature generation as a foundational *research data* challenge rather than a mere administrative utility, and fully comply with the 2026 AI disclosure policies.14  
**Phase 3: Sector Dissemination and Conferencing (Months 9–12)** With a preprint DOI secured and peer review underway, grassroots promotion should commence across BAJR forums and CIfA networks. Abstracts must be submitted to the CAA 2026 and EAA 2026 (Athens) conferences to demonstrate the tool to key institutional decision-makers and potential enterprise adopters.45 Concurrently, applications for technology-focused grants, specifically the AIA Steinmetz Endowment (due November 1, 2026), should be aggressively pursued to fund the subsequent iteration, UX design, and long-term maintenance of the pipeline.54  
By intelligently bridging the rigorous requirements of open-source software validation with the highly specific, practical realities of commercial heritage workflows, HOARD is uniquely positioned to modernize archaeological reporting. Through strategic publication, targeted conferencing, and the leveraging of offline AI for absolute data privacy, HOARD can transition from a localized script into a foundational infrastructural tool for the global heritage sector.
