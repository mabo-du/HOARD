# **Automated Generation of Archaeological Grey Literature: Data Sources, Synthetic Architectures, and Evaluation Metrics**

## **Introduction to the Data Integration Imperative in Archaeology**

The discipline of archaeology is fundamentally defined by its destructive nature; once a site is excavated, it ceases to exist in its original physical form and is preserved solely through the rigorous documentation of its excavation. The subsequent synthesis of this primary field data—ranging from raw context sheets, finds registers, and environmental samples to complex stratigraphic matrices—into cohesive, narrative grey literature reports represents the absolute cornerstone of professional archaeological practice. The global heritage sector is currently grappling with a profound curation crisis, characterized by an immense backlog of unpublished or under-synthesized field data, often colloquially termed legacy data. The integration of artificial intelligence, specifically local large language models (LLMs) and structured natural language generation (NLG) pipelines, into this workflow promises a paradigm shift in how the discipline manages its escalating data debt. However, the architectural design of a system such as the HOARD pipeline, which aims to automatically translate tabular field data into publishable grey literature, requires an evaluation framework of unprecedented rigor.  
Developing automated pipelines for scientific and technical document generation demands an evaluation methodology far more stringent than those utilized for general-purpose text generators. Archaeological documentation is bound by strict jurisdictional compliance standards, precise stratigraphic logic governed by the Harris Matrix, and an absolute intolerance for factual hallucination. A generated report that lexically resembles a professional document but inverses a stratigraphic relationship—for example, stating that an intrusive pit is physically cut by a later medieval wall rather than the reverse—represents a catastrophic algorithmic failure. Evaluating the efficacy of these text-generation pipelines requires the assembly of robust, ground-truth integration test datasets that pair raw input data (context-level databases) directly with human-authored output (published reports) for end-to-end validation.  
This comprehensive report investigates the availability of open archaeological datasets across global repositories to identify paired raw and published records. It further outlines methodologies for the generation of synthetic, yet highly realistic, test data aligned with major jurisdictional templates when empirical datasets prove insufficient. Crucially, the report defines an exhaustive automated quality metric framework—detailing the severe limitations of standard natural language processing metrics such as ROUGE-L—and constructs a practical, three-tiered integration test suite for the complete algorithmic validation of automated archaeological report generation pipelines.

## **The Landscape of Open Archaeological Datasets**

The global transition toward digital archiving has produced massive, distributed repositories of archaeological data. However, the paradigm of treating data sharing as equivalent to academic publication remains unevenly implemented across different global jurisdictions and commercial archaeology sectors. In a vast majority of instances, the final interpretive grey literature report is separated from the raw tabular databases that originally informed its creation. This detachment creates a significant hurdle for training and evaluating data-to-text artificial intelligence models, which require absolute alignment between the structured input and the narrative output. The following sections explore the primary data repositories across the United Kingdom, the United States, and Australia to identify suitable paired datasets.

### **United Kingdom Data Architectures: The Archaeology Data Service**

The Archaeology Data Service (ADS) serves as the primary digital repository for heritage data in the United Kingdom, operating under the CoreTrustSeal accreditation and maintaining over 57,000 grey literature reports. The repository is heavily populated through the OASIS (Online Access to the Index of archaeological investigations) system, which mandates the deposition of reports by commercial archaeological units. While the ADS library is unparalleled in its volume of narrative reports, discovering paired, context-level databases requires navigating deeply into individual, unstructured project archives.  
Historically, commercial archaeology in the United Kingdom has prioritized the deposition of the finalized report, frequently submitted as a flattened PDF file, over the raw digital archive. This practice is gradually shifting due to updated professional guidelines, but legacy archives often lack digital primary records. Datasets within the ADS that successfully pair both elements tend to originate from large-scale academic research excavations, leveraged by substantial funding bodies, or major civil infrastructure projects. Notable examples include the Silchester Roman Town (Insula IX) project, which provides highly granular context databases alongside exceptionally detailed narrative publications. Similarly, multi-site academic initiatives, such as the dataset covering living standards and material culture in English rural households, provide extensive relational database files, entity relationship tables, and corresponding analytical reports.  
Finding simple, single-site commercial evaluations, such as routine watching briefs, that feature both the raw context records in a tabular format (such as CSV or Microsoft Access) and the final narrative report requires highly targeted search parameters within the ADS Archives division. Researchers must filter by specific event types, such as "Archaeological Intervention," and specifically cross-reference the presence of tabular data with PDF fieldwork reports. Furthermore, differing terminology and idiosyncratic database structures across different commercial units hinder meaningful cross-searching of these excavation datasets, presenting a significant preprocessing challenge for pipeline integration.

### **United States and Global Infrastructures: Open Context and tDAR**

In the United States, digital archaeological repositories generally follow two distinct architectural philosophies, fundamentally impacting how their data can be ingested and utilized within local LLM pipelines.  
Open Context represents a highly granular, entity-level database framework that publishes archaeological research data, field notes, and typologies from across the globe, including significant deposits from the United Kingdom and the Mediterranean. The platform utilizes linked open data approaches to integrate specific entities—such as individual context sheets, faunal remains, and ceramic sherds—into a mutually intelligible, semantic web of data. Open Context editors actively clean and normalize data during the editorial workflow, mitigating the spelling variations, abbreviations, and typographic errors commonly found in raw, untreated field notes. The repository frequently features digital context sheets and field diaries in easily downloadable tabular formats, making it an exceptional resource for sourcing highly structured, deeply cleaned input data. The platform's emphasis on interoperability at the entity level means that users can download individual observations transposed from context sheets directly into CSV formats, providing an ideal input schema for data-to-text generation.  
In contrast, the Digital Archaeological Record (tDAR), supported by Digital Antiquity, operates predominantly at the collection and document level. While tDAR hosts vast amounts of legacy reports and databases, its primary emphasis on interoperability has focused on high-level information exchanges and ontology alignment with other peer repositories, rather than granular, entity-level linked data. The raw data hosted on tDAR is frequently encapsulated within project-specific, localized spreadsheet structures. For example, the extensive Pueblo Grande excavation datasets conducted by Soil Systems, Inc. include detailed provenience designation sheets and feature coding tables that rely heavily on proprietary alphanumeric codes. While tDAR is rich in complex, multi-layered site data, utilizing this information for pipeline testing requires substantial preprocessing to map the proprietary codes back to natural language descriptions before the data can be effectively understood by a language model.

### **Australian Regulatory Portals and Heritage Architectures**

The landscape of open-access raw archaeological data in Australia is currently far less centralized than the digital infrastructures present in the United Kingdom or the United States. State Heritage Authority portals and specific academic platforms, such as NSW Archaeology Online, provide exceptional access to historical grey literature, conservation management plans, and archaeological assessment reports. However, these repositories function almost exclusively as document libraries rather than integrated repositories of raw, tabular context data.  
Under legislative frameworks such as the Aboriginal Heritage Act 2006, bodies like Heritage Victoria require the mandatory submission of Site Cards, Notices of Intention to Undertake an Archaeological Survey, and comprehensive survey reports for any archaeological discovery. While these site cards contain structured metadata regarding the site's significance and spatial coordinates, accessing the underlying, raw field data—such as context sheets or finds catalogues—for algorithmic training remains exceptionally challenging without direct institutional data-sharing agreements.  
Similarly, databases such as AustArch, which compiles radiocarbon and non-radiocarbon ages from archaeological sites across Australia, are highly specialized analytical tools rather than repositories of primary stratigraphic field recording. Consequently, relying on Australian open data portals to assemble an integration test suite of raw context sheets paired with narrative reports is currently not a viable strategy without conducting primary, manual digitization of legacy physical archives.

### **Open Access Journals and Specialized Data Publishing Platforms**

Globally, the push for transparent open data is championed by specialized academic journals such as the Journal of Open Archaeology Data and Internet Archaeology. These platforms explicitly endorse open science frameworks, such as the Panton Principles, and utilize Creative Commons licenses to ensure all content is freely available. The Journal of Open Archaeology Data enforces the publication of specific "data papers" that mandate the deposition of raw datasets in trusted repositories, such as Zenodo, alongside comprehensive methodological metadata.  
These platforms are invaluable for locating highly curated, academically rigorous datasets. However, the data published through these avenues often leans heavily toward specialized, derivative analytical data—such as zooarchaeological quantifications, chemical and metallographic analyses of artifacts, or multi-period landscape survey data—rather than the standard commercial context sheets required for grey literature report generation. While Internet Archaeology occasionally hosts deeply integrated digital archives that link excavation photographs and context sheets directly to the published text, the vast majority of the published data represents the final synthesis of the site, rather than the raw, primary stratigraphic record required to train a data-to-text model.

### **Document Processing and Handwritten Text Recognition Frameworks**

The automated transcription of legacy paper records remains a critical bottleneck in archaeological data science. Frameworks such as Transkribus, HTRflow, and eScriptorium leverage advanced Convolutional Neural Networks and Handwritten Text Recognition algorithms to digitize archival documents. While there are extensive, high-quality open datasets available for training models on historical manuscripts—such as early modern Spanish scripts, 18th-century Danish administrative writing, or French dailies—there is currently a profound lack of open, pre-trained Handwritten Text Recognition datasets specifically curated for archaeological context sheets.  
Context sheets pose unique, highly complex machine-vision challenges. They are not continuous prose; rather, they are a chaotic mixture of structured tabular grids, unstructured free-text descriptions, specialized stratigraphic vocabulary, physical site sketch plans, and mathematical level matrices. Datasets trained by prominent research groups demonstrate high character accuracy rates on running text, but applying these generalized models to the erratic layouts of archaeological field recording forms results in severe degradation of output quality. While specialized workflows, such as those developed by the Atrium research project, attempt to extract entities from transcribed archaeological context sheets, they still rely on the initial, manual establishment of ground truth. Consequently, relying on pre-existing Handwritten Text Recognition datasets to instantly assemble a test suite of raw context sheets is not feasible; any integration of scanned context sheets into a pipeline currently necessitates bespoke, resource-intensive model training and manual transcription validation.

## **Architecting Synthetic Test Data for Archaeological Contexts**

Given the scarcity of perfectly aligned, cleanly structured open datasets that represent the entire spectrum of archaeological complexity, generating synthetic but highly realistic test data emerges as a necessary and highly effective strategy for robust software evaluation. Synthetic data generation allows developers to exert total control over the input variables, enabling the deliberate injection of edge cases, stratigraphic anomalies, severe truncation events, and incomplete data records. This controlled environment pushes an automated generation pipeline to its absolute analytical limits, ensuring it can handle the chaotic reality of actual field data without failing silently or producing hallucinated narratives.

### **Adherence to Jurisdictional Templates and Standards**

Creating synthetic archaeological data requires far more than randomly generating strings of text; it requires strict adherence to established professional recording standards to ensure the generated inputs perfectly mimic real-world field data. In the United Kingdom, the Historic England Archaeological Recording Manual provides the definitive, structural framework for data capture. This manual dictates the strict atomization of data across distinct pro-forma sheets specifically designed for Cuts, Deposits, Masonry, and Timber structures.  
To synthesize data effectively, one must computationally replicate the schema of these templates. A standard Historic England CL3-style context sheet, or the equivalent forms provided by major commercial units, includes specific, mandatory data fields. These fields encompass the Context Number, Context Type (Cut, Fill, Layer, or Structure), Stratigraphic Relationships (Above, Below, Cuts, Cut By), Physical Description (Color, Compaction, Composition, Inclusions), Dimensions (Length, Width, Depth), and the excavator's provisional Interpretation. Generating synthetic data involves populating these highly structured fields using randomized, yet logically coherent, domain-specific variables.  
Blank templates and procedural guidelines to base synthetic generators upon are readily accessible within the public domain. Commercial organizations frequently publish their open-access recording sheets and registers, defining the standard data constraints required for valid recording. Furthermore, resources such as the British Archaeological Jobs and Resource network provide comprehensive report templates detailing the required structural hierarchy for standard grey literature. This includes the mandatory inclusion of a Non-Technical Summary, Introduction, Methodology, Stratigraphic Sequence, Finds Analysis, and Conclusion. In the United States, similar structural rigidities apply to Section 106 compliance reports, while in Wales, Cadw guidelines dictate the necessary reporting standards. A synthetic data generator must be capable of mapping its outputs to fulfill the specific structural requirements of these diverse jurisdictional templates.

### **Procedural Generation via CIDOC CRM and Graph Logic**

The primary, overarching challenge in generating synthetic archaeological data is maintaining absolute stratigraphic integrity. Randomly generating context sheets will inevitably result in physical impossibilities—such as a deposit being chronologically recorded as earlier than the cut it fills, or two separate features cutting each other in a temporal paradox.  
To overcome this fundamental issue, the synthetic generation process must be anchored by a computationally generated Harris Matrix. A procedural generation algorithm designed for this purpose should operate through a specific sequence of operations. First, it must generate a valid directed acyclic graph (DAG) representing the Harris Matrix, ensuring mathematically that no circular dependencies or temporal paradoxes exist within the sequence. Second, it must assign broad temporal phases to the nodes within the graph, establishing a chronological framework. Third, it must instantiate specific context types based on standard node relationships. For example, a terminal node with no subsequent physical relationships is logically designated as a modern topsoil layer, whereas a node with a strict one-to-one sequential relationship is highly likely to be a subsequent fill within an earlier cut. Finally, the algorithm must populate the descriptive fields of the synthetic context sheets based on domain-specific ontological dictionaries, utilizing standardized terminologies such as Munsell color charts for soil descriptions and standard inclusion types like ceramic building material or charcoal.  
Crucially, this procedurally generated data must then be mapped to standardized, international ontologies such as the CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model (CRM). As demonstrated by the Semantic Technologies for Archaeological Resources (STAR) and STELLAR projects, mapping synthetic or legacy datasets to the CIDOC CRM enables semantic integration across entirely different database schemas. This ontological alignment ensures that the synthetically generated data remains fully interoperable, machine-readable, and capable of being ingested seamlessly by natural language processing pipelines designed to handle disparate real-world data structures.

## **Automated Quality Metrics for Technical Report Generation**

The automated evaluation of artificial intelligence-generated scientific and technical texts requires moving far beyond the standard assessment metrics utilized for general conversational chatbots. The generated archaeological report must be evaluated across three uncompromising dimensions: Content Accuracy (the absolute fidelity to the raw input data), Compliance (the strict adherence to jurisdictional and structural reporting guidelines), and Hallucination Detection (the verifiable absence of fabricated facts, invented artifacts, or contradictory stratigraphic statements).

### **Content Accuracy and the Limitations of Lexical Metrics**

Historically, the Natural Language Generation community has relied heavily on traditional metrics like ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) and BLEU to measure the statistical similarity between a piece of generated text and a human-written reference document. ROUGE-L, in particular, measures the Longest Common Subsequence between two texts, attempting to capture sentence-level structure without requiring consecutive, exact matches.  
However, deploying ROUGE-L to evaluate technical archaeological reporting is a deeply flawed methodology due to its fundamental semantic blindness. ROUGE operates strictly on surface-level lexical overlap; it possesses entirely no capacity to capture semantic meaning, logical entailment, or contextual understanding. Consider the following two sentences describing a basic stratigraphic relationship:  
*Reference Ground Truth:* "Context 102 is a substantial V-shaped ditch which cuts the natural geology (Context 103)."  
*Generated Output:* "Context 103 is a substantial V-shaped ditch which cuts the natural geology (Context 102)."  
In this example, the generated text inverses the archaeological reality, fundamentally changing the physical interpretation of the site and rendering the report scientifically invalid. Yet, because the exact same words are used and only their order is slightly transposed, ROUGE-L will return an exceptionally high score, completely missing the catastrophic factual error. Furthermore, ROUGE-L actively penalizes valid, accurate paraphrasing. If a model generates the phrase "yellowish-brown silty clay" instead of the exact reference phrase "brown-yellow clay silt," it receives a lower accuracy score despite the descriptions being archaeologically and pedologically synonymous. Additionally, the broader natural language processing community faces a severe reproducibility crisis regarding ROUGE implementations, with studies indicating that up to seventy-six percent of academic citations reference software with unacknowledged scoring errors or undocumented configuration parameters.  
To address these limitations, variants such as ROUGE-SS have been proposed, which attempt to incorporate synonym matching into the evaluation. Semantic similarity metrics, such as BERTScore, offer a more robust improvement by utilizing highly contextualized embeddings to measure the cosine similarity of tokens, rather than relying on exact string matching. While BERTScore is significantly better at recognizing synonyms and subtle paraphrasing, it still struggles with the rigorous, rigid logic of stratigraphy, spatial relationships, and quantitative artifact counts.

### **Compliance Validation and Semantic Assessment**

Compliance evaluation dictates whether the generated report successfully adheres to the specific, rigid templates demanded by regional heritage authorities. This dimension of evaluation can be divided into automated rule-based verification and more complex semantic evaluation.  
Rule-based verification utilizes automated scripts to efficiently perform checks without relying on computationally expensive neural networks. These deterministic checks evaluate the structural skeleton of the document. They validate the presence of mandatory sections by scanning for specific headers. They enforce quantitative thresholds, ensuring that overall word counts meet minimum professional standards and checking the ratio of narrative text to data tables. Rule-based checks are also critical for metadata inclusion, confirming that essential project details—such as national grid references, site codes, and event dates—are prominently extracted from the input data and correctly formatted at the beginning of the report. Finally, these scripts scan for prohibited terminology, flagging the use of unscientific, colloquial, or outdated language that violates modern reporting standards, such as the use of the word "dirt" instead of the scientifically accurate "soil" or "deposit."  
Semantic evaluation, however, requires assessing whether the actual content of the generated sections fulfills their intended analytical purpose. For example, it must ensure that the generated "Methodology" section correctly identifies whether the physical excavation was a localized watching brief, a linear trench evaluation, or an open-area excavation, based solely on inferring the spatial distribution of the context data. This step generally requires an LLM-as-a-judge framework, utilizing advanced techniques like G-Eval. In this paradigm, a secondary, highly capable language model is provided with a strict evaluation rubric and prompted to generate structured reasoning steps to score the output against the specific compliance criteria.

### **Hallucination Detection in Structured Data-to-Text Generation**

Hallucinations in large language models—defined as fluent, syntactically correct, but entirely fabricated or unsupported information—pose the single greatest threat to the adoption of automated scientific writing. In the highly specific domain of archaeological data-to-text generation, a hallucination might manifest as the spontaneous invention of an artifact not present in the tabular finds database, the assertion of a physical stratigraphic relationship that directly contradicts the raw input matrix, or the fabrication of an incorrect historical date range for a specific ceramic typology. Detecting these domain-specific hallucinations requires specialized, advanced architectural approaches.  
The SelfCheckGPT framework provides a zero-resource, black-box approach that detects hallucinations by leveraging the principle of model consistency. The framework queries the language model multiple times to generate multiple, independent responses to the exact same input data. It then algorithmically measures the informational overlap and consistency between these stochastic samples. Factual, grounded information will consistently appear across all generated samples, whereas hallucinated, fabricated details will vary wildly from generation to generation. While highly effective—especially when utilizing its specific Natural Language Inference variants—SelfCheckGPT is exceptionally computationally expensive, requiring massive token generation overhead for a single report.  
Natural Language Inference (NLI) using local models represents a highly effective, logical approach to hallucination detection. NLI models evaluate pairs of sentences—treating the raw structured input data as the premise and the generated narrative text as the hypothesis—and strictly classify their relationship as Entailment, Contradiction, or Neutral. Recent advancements have demonstrated that lightweight, open-weight local models, when fine-tuned or prompted with Chain-of-Thought reasoning, excel at complex NLI tasks. For example, models such as LLaMA-3 8B, running entirely locally, have been shown to achieve state-of-the-art factual detection scores, outperforming even massive proprietary models on strict logical entailment tasks. Deploying a local LLaMA model specifically tasked with verifying the generated text against the original JSON or CSV input on a line-by-line basis is a highly robust, privacy-preserving method for identifying contradictory statements.  
Furthermore, cutting-edge, computationally lightweight interventions seek to detect hallucinations by analyzing the latent space and internal architecture of the models during the generation process itself. The Truthfulness Separator Vector (TSV) is a plug-and-play steering vector that operates dynamically during inference. By subtly altering the model's internal representation space, it forces a much clearer mathematical separation between truthful and hallucinated outputs, allowing for high-accuracy detection without the need to alter or retrain the base model's parameters. Similarly, analyzing the spectral features of the model's internal attention maps can reveal hallucinations; when a model retrieves grounded, factual information, the eigenvalues of its attention maps display stable structures, whereas fabricating data results in chaotic, diffuse patterns that can be immediately flagged. Frameworks like the Entity Tracing Framework (ETF), initially designed for hallucination detection in code summarization, can also be adapted to trace specific entities—such as context numbers or artifact IDs—from the raw data directly through to the generated text, ensuring no unsupported entities are introduced into the narrative.

## **Practical Test Dataset Assembly and Preprocessing Integration**

To validate the HOARD pipeline fully from end to end, the integration test suite must cover varied levels of real-world archaeological complexity. The following three datasets have been meticulously selected from the available open repositories discussed above. They represent a logical progression from simple, localized urban watching briefs to immensely complex, multi-phase academic excavations. For each tier, explicit preprocessing steps are defined to convert the raw repository files into the structured formats required for language model ingestion.

### **Tier 1: Simple Urban Monitoring and Evaluation**

For the initial pipeline integration and baseline testing, a small, well-documented dataset featuring a limited number of contexts and a straightforward, linear narrative report is required. This tier tests the pipeline's ability to generate standard commercial boilerplate text without over-interpreting sparse data.  
**Dataset:** Shirley Towers and Milner Court, Southampton (SOU 1728\)

* **Repository Source:** Archaeology Data Service (United Kingdom).  
* **Site Description:** An archaeological watching brief conducted by Southampton City Council. The intervention was small-scale and highly localized, consisting primarily of monitoring geotechnical test pits and boreholes. The resulting dataset is concise, focusing almost exclusively on modern to post-medieval demolition deposits and made ground. This makes it an ideal baseline to test whether the natural language generation model can generate a succinct, standard commercial report without hallucinating complex prehistoric features.  
* **Available Files:** Tabular databases detailing specific deposits and objects, accompanied by the finalized, fully published PDF grey literature report.  
* **Direct URL:** https://archaeologydataservice.ac.uk/archives/view/1004182/index.cfm (Alternatively accessible by searching "SOU 1728" in the ADS ArchSearch interface).  
* **License and Access Requirements:** Open access via standard ADS terms of use; no account registration is required for data download.

**HOARD Preprocessing Integration Steps:**

1. Download the primary context and object databases, which are typically provided by the ADS as standard CSV files or Microsoft Access database tables.  
2. Execute a mapping script to align the raw column headers provided by the excavator (such as "Deposit," "Inclusions," and "Depth") directly to HOARD's internal, standardized JSON or CSV input schema.  
3. Extract the plain text from the accompanying PDF grey literature report. This extracted text will serve as the absolute ground truth reference for automated evaluation, allowing for baseline comparisons using BERTScore and NLI models against the generated output.

### **Tier 2: Medium Complexity Testing and Data Recovery**

A medium complexity dataset must feature a significantly greater density of intercutting features, distinct chronological phasing, and specialized artifact datasets (such as detailed ceramic typologies or faunal remains). This tier forces the generation pipeline to synthesize multiple disparate lines of evidence into a single, coherent analytical narrative.  
**Dataset:** Pueblo Grande (AZ U:9:1(ASM)): Unit 13, NE Corner of Van Buren and 44th Streets: SSI AZ Federal Credit Union Testing

* **Repository Source:** The Digital Archaeological Record (tDAR) (United States).  
* **Site Description:** Conducted by Soil Systems, Inc., this dataset represents a major testing and data recovery project at a large, highly complex Hohokam village in Arizona. The dataset includes the crucial "Provenience Designation (PD) Data" sheet, which acts as the master relational list for all excavated contexts. It contains highly specific vertical locations, relative stratigraphic positions, and complex feature records, providing a robust, multi-layered stratigraphic challenge for the algorithm.  
* **Available Files:** Comprehensive tabular data covering hundreds of contexts, recovery methods, absolute elevations, and artifact collections, paired with associated textual documentation and metadata.  
* **Direct URL:** https://core.tdar.org/dataset/372325/pueblo-grande-az-u91asm-unit-13-ne-corner-of-van-buren-and-44th-streets-ssi-az-federal-credit-union-testing-provenience-designation-pd-data  
* **License and Access Requirements:** Free access; however, it requires the creation of a free tDAR user account to initiate the download of the raw data files.

**HOARD Preprocessing Integration Steps:**

1. Download the primary "PD\_DATA" tables and all associated Context Coding Sheets from the tDAR repository.  
2. Execute data decoding algorithms. The raw files from this specific commercial unit utilize proprietary, coded numeric values for context definitions (for example, using specific integer codes to represent distinct soil types or inclusion densities). The HOARD preprocessor must computationally join these raw tables with the provided lookup dictionaries to convert all integer codes back into natural language descriptions (e.g., translating a specific code into "silty clay with heavy charcoal inclusions") prior to ingestion by the language model.  
3. Structure the input data chronologically or by distinct feature groups to rigorously test the pipeline's ability to logically partition the generated report into coherent, distinct chapters.

### **Tier 3: High Complexity Academic Excavation**

To test the absolute limits of the language model's analytical capacity and context window memory, the pipeline must successfully process thousands of interconnected context records spanning multiple centuries of complex occupational history, accompanied by extensive, highly technical specialist reports.  
**Dataset:** The Silchester Project: Roman Town Insula IX (The Development of an Urban Property c. AD 40-50 \- c. AD 250\)

* **Repository Source:** Archaeology Data Service (United Kingdom).  
* **Site Description:** Directed by the University of Reading, this represents one of the most comprehensively digitized, granular archaeological datasets available globally. It covers the highly complex transition of an Iron Age oppidum into a fully structured Roman town. The database contains thousands of individually recorded, deeply detailed contexts (e.g., Context 4188: a silty accumulation deposit over clay, explicitly cut by feature 1035), paired with thousands of small finds records. The associated multi-volume academic monographs serve as the absolute pinnacle of published interpretive literature against which to test the model.  
* **Available Files:** Highly interactive and directly downloadable CSV context databases, exhaustive finds databases, digital plans, and comprehensive narrative academic reports.  
* **Direct URL:** https://archaeologydataservice.ac.uk/archives/view/silchester\_ahrc\_2007/  
* **License and Access Requirements:** Open access via standard ADS terms of use.

**HOARD Preprocessing Integration Steps:**

1. Extract the massive context database, ensuring all fields containing physical descriptions, inclusions, specific Munsell colors, and the exact, critical stratigraphic relationships (Above, Below, Cuts, Cut By) are perfectly preserved.  
2. Given the sheer volume of context data, the HOARD pipeline must implement a sophisticated algorithmic grouping preprocessing step. The raw data must be programmatically clustered by Phase and Group (e.g., aggregating all contexts related to "Phase 2, Timber Structure B") before being fed into the language model's context window. Attempting to feed thousands of raw context rows into an LLM simultaneously will exceed token limits, degrade the model's attention mechanisms, and induce severe, uncontrollable hallucination.  
3. Utilize an external graph database algorithm to build the definitive Harris Matrix from the raw relational data fields prior to text generation. This explicit matrix sequence must then be fed to the language model to provide an unalterable, mathematical structural spine for the narrative generation, absolutely ensuring that the model cannot violate the established physical stratigraphy during its prose generation.
