# The HOARD Pipeline in 2026: A Strategic Guide to Upgrading Local AI Models for Archaeological Reporting

This report provides a comprehensive deep-dive analysis for updating the Heritage Observation And Report Drafter (HOARD) pipeline as of June 2026. The research evaluates the rapidly evolving landscape of open-weight artificial intelligence models against the pipeline's specific requirements, including stringent hardware constraints (≤6GB VRAM), the need for specialized performance in archaeological contexts, and a commitment to privacy through fully local execution. The analysis is structured across four key domains: an evaluation of newly released sub-8 billion parameter language models for each of the five pipeline phases; a detailed comparison of vision-language models (VLMs) for digitizing documents and interpreting images; an examination of best practices for orchestrating multi-model workflows on constrained consumer-grade hardware using Ollama v0.5+; and a direct comparative benchmark of local models against leading cloud-based API services. The final section synthesizes these findings into a set of actionable recommendations for maintaining and enhancing the strategic value of the HOARD pipeline.

## Sub-8B Open-Weight Language Model Landscape Analysis

The selection of appropriate Small Language Models (SLMs) is a critical component of the HOARD pipeline's evolution, as these models must operate within strict VRAM limitations while delivering high-quality outputs relevant to technical archaeological prose [[55](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ascendancy-small-language-models-slms-2026-rohan-pinto-4ccnc)]. The landscape of open-weight SLMs has seen significant advancements since May 2026, with new families emerging that prioritize architectural refinement and data quality over sheer parameter counts [[1](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/piyush-ranjan-9297a632_the-11-best-open-source-llms-for-2026-activity-7448361664292360192-jMCG), [43](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.03350v1)]. This section analyzes these developments, evaluating candidates against the core HOARD requirements: permissive licensing (Apache 2.0, MIT), a minimum 32K token context window, a ≤6GB VRAM footprint at 4-bit quantization, and demonstrated relevance to archaeological and technical documentation. The analysis reveals a nuanced environment where no single model is superior for all tasks; instead, an optimal strategy involves selecting specialized models for specific pipeline phases.

The most prominent trend in the SLM space is the ascendancy of models developed with high intentionality, focusing on data quality and efficient architectures rather than just scaling up parameters [[55](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/ascendancy-small-language-models-slms-2026-rohan-pinto-4ccnc)]. This shift is exemplified by the rise of several new model families. DeepSeek-V3, for instance, has been positioned as a top-tier open-weight model, reportedly outperforming even commercial giants like GPT-4o on certain benchmarks [[37](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/new-leader-open-source-ai-deepseek-v3-benchmarking-1sryc), [38](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401014414_Performance_of_GPT-4o_GEMINI_25_Pro_Claude_Sonnet_4_And_Deepseek_V3_Models_in_Coding)]. Crucially, its planned release under the Apache 2.0 license makes it highly suitable for the HOARD project's permissive licensing requirement [[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2306.02781v4)]. Similarly, Microsoft's Phi-4, a 14-billion parameter model, was developed with a training recipe centrally focused on data quality, a principle directly transferable to the structured and precise nature of archaeological writing [[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08905v1), [40](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/phi-4-technical-report/)]. The Qwen family continues its iterative development, with Qwen3 being a recent release that builds upon previous versions [[74](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.09388)], though the current HOARD model, Qwen3.5-4B, is already part of this lineage [[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-10097-9)]. Google's Gemma series remains a strong institutional baseline, particularly its smaller E-series models, which have been noted for use in heritage practice and align with the efficiency required for compliance tasks [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/374228171_Large_Language_Models_for_Cultural_Heritage), [77](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.13811)].

A primary challenge in evaluating these new models is the lack of publicly available, standardized information regarding their exact context window sizes. The HOARD pipeline's requirement for a minimum 32K token context window is non-negotiable, as it is necessary to process full site datasets comprehensively [[47](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~ark/blog-data/data/blog_data_v1_0/dk/hbc_data/data/cmnt_vocab.txt)]. Without this specific data point for each candidate model, a definitive recommendation cannot be made, and it must be considered a critical verification step prior to any implementation. The provided sources do not contain explicit figures for many of the newer models, leaving this a significant gap in the current analysis. However, some models like Claude Sonnet 3.5 and GPT-4o are known to support very large context windows (e.g., 200K tokens and 128K tokens respectively), setting a high bar for what is technologically feasible [[93](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/vivek-panday_tokenoptimization-aiengineering-llm-activity-7430859389160558592-pr2V)]. It is reasonable to assume that state-of-the-art open-source models from mid-2026 would offer comparable or better capabilities, but this remains an assumption without direct evidence.

In terms of VRAM footprint, quantization is the key enabler for running larger models on consumer hardware. A 4-bit quantized model significantly reduces both disk space and VRAM requirements, allowing more complex models to run on less powerful GPUs [[60](https://dev.to/mitchell_cheng/my-learning-notes-choosing-the-right-ai-model-and-hardware-237)]. While specific numbers for every candidate are unavailable, we can make informed estimates based on established principles. A 4-bit quantized 4-billion parameter model like the current Qwen3.5-4B is expected to comfortably fit within the 6GB limit [[27](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ben-mcfarlin_qwen-aiengineering-llm-activity-7450998962796298241-Xw-n)]. Larger models, such as a 7-billion parameter DeepSeek-V3 or a 14-billion parameter Phi-4, would require careful consideration. A 4-bit quantized 7B model would likely remain within the 6GB budget, making it a viable candidate. A 14B model would be more challenging; a 4-bit version might push the limits of the 6GB constraint, potentially requiring a fallback to a lower-precision quantization or limiting concurrent processes. The choice of quantization level itself presents a trade-off: Q4_K_M offers the smallest footprint, while Q5_K_M provides a slight accuracy improvement, and Q8 preserves the most detail at the highest resource cost [[60](https://dev.to/mitchell_cheng/my-learning-notes-choosing-the-right-ai-model-and-hardware-237)]. For archaeological prose, which relies on precision and correct terminology, the higher fidelity of Q5_K_M or even Q8 may be justified for critical synthesis and compliance phases, even if it means sacrificing throughput or requiring more robust memory management.

Based on this preliminary analysis, a phased upgrade strategy for the HOARD pipeline's language models is recommended. The current model for Phase 4, Gemma 4-E2B, appears well-suited to its task of jurisdiction-specific restructuring due to its small size and proven utility in institutional settings [[77](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.13811)]. There is little immediate incentive to replace it unless a newer, more capable variant becomes available. For Phase 3 (Synthesis & Drafting), the current Qwen3.5-4B serves as a reliable baseline [[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-10097-9)]. However, given the reported performance of DeepSeek-V3, it represents the strongest potential upgrade candidate. A direct comparative test between Qwen3.5-4B and DeepSeek-V3 on a representative dataset of archaeological reports is the logical next step. If DeepSeek-V3 meets the VRAM and context window requirements, it should be adopted for Phase 3. For Phase 1 (Digitisation), the choice is less clear. While general-purpose models like Phi-4 are excellent, the task may benefit from a model specifically fine-tuned on historical or handwritten documents, a capability highlighted in recent research [[5](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/333156701_A_Set_of_Benchmarks_for_Handwritten_Text_Recognition_on_Historical_Documents), [30](https://academic.oup.com/dsh/article/41/1/22/8407498)]. The existence of benchmarks like TimeTravel, designed for AI-driven analysis of manuscripts and archaeological discoveries, underscores the importance of domain-specific training [[97](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-acl.1211.pdf)]. Therefore, before committing to a new generalist SLM, it would be prudent to investigate whether a fine-tuned version of an existing model or a new model specializing in document analysis offers a better solution. The final choice for each phase should be guided by a combination of objective benchmark scores, subjective evaluation of prose quality on archaeological texts, and empirical testing against the VRAM constraints of the target hardware [[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507), [12](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0305440326001019)].

## Multimodal VLM Evaluation for Archaeological Document and Image Understanding

The first two phases of the HOARD pipeline rely heavily on Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to convert physical fieldwork artifacts into machine-readable data. Phase 1 involves recognizing handwritten text on context sheets, a notoriously difficult task due to poor handwriting, stains, and complex form layouts. Phase 2 requires generating descriptive captions for site photographs, understanding stratigraphic sections, and identifying spatial relationships between features and artefacts. Evaluating the June 2026 open-weight VLM landscape against these specific challenges is crucial for determining whether the current models—GLM-OCR for Phase 1 and Qwen3-VL-8B for Phase 2—remain optimal.

The primary challenge in Phase 1 is not merely Optical Character Recognition (OCR) but "document intelligence," which encompasses layout analysis, reading order detection, and semantic interpretation of form fields [[4](https://openreview.net/pdf/e9eaf3d533ddb4c4edd16142a51fbe39cb9244a7.pdf), [69](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/357124534_LayoutReader_Pre-training_of_Text_and_Layout_for_Reading_Order_Detection)]. The incumbent model, GLM-OCR, excels at character-level recognition but may struggle with the higher-level reasoning needed to interpret a grid-based form with checkboxes and contextual clues [[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08746v1)]. Its main weakness is treating the document as a collection of images rather than a structured entity [[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08746v1)]. To address this, newer VLMs are increasingly incorporating layout-aware processing. Florence-2, for example, is explicitly designed with capabilities for reading order prediction and layout analysis, making it exceptionally well-suited for interpreting messy, grid-formatted context sheets [[69](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/357124534_LayoutReader_Pre-training_of_Text_and_Layout_for_Reading_Order_Detection), [80](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/WACV2024/papers/Feinglass_Towards_Addressing_the_Misalignment_of_Object_Proposal_Evaluation_for_Vision-Language_WACV_2024_paper.pdf)]. Similarly, InternVL2 demonstrates strong multimodal parsing abilities, particularly for visually-rich documents, positioning it as another prime candidate for improving Phase 1 accuracy [[50](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353486053_LayoutLMv2_Multi-modal_Pre-training_for_Visually-rich_Document_Understanding), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.19760v1)]. An architecture like DocVLM, which integrates OCR as a distinct modality into the VLM without altering its original weights, offers a conceptual framework for a hybrid approach: using a powerful OCR engine (potentially enhanced with image pre-processing techniques [[31](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/9480013/image-processing-to-improve-tesseract-ocr-accuracy)]) to extract clean text blocks, and then feeding this structured data along with positional coordinates to a VLM like Florence-2 for final interpretation [[4](https://openreview.net/pdf/e9eaf3d533ddb4c4edd16142a51fbe39cb9244a7.pdf), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08746v1)]. This two-stage process could significantly improve the accuracy of extracting data from forms with checkboxes and varying handwriting quality [[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.20220v1)].

For Phase 2, the requirements shift from document structure to spatial and visual reasoning. The current model, Qwen3-VL-8B, is tasked with captioning site photos and understanding spatial relationships. While its performance is established, the rapid advancement in VLMs suggests that newer alternatives could offer improved fidelity. PaliGemma 2, an upgrade built on the Gemma 2 LLM, represents a promising candidate [[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.03555v1)]. Its connection to the Gemma family implies stronger language generation capabilities, which could translate into more accurate and detailed captions that use proper archaeological terminology [[52](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/374228171_Large_Language_Models_for_Cultural_Heritage)]. Other models like MiniCPM-V and InternVL2 also show strong performance in multimodal understanding and could serve as effective replacements [[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507)]. However, it is critical to acknowledge a fundamental limitation of current VLMs: their difficulty with basic geometric understanding. One study found that VLMs often fail to accurately localize simple shapes like squares, a deficiency that would be a critical failure when analyzing stratigraphic plans or feature diagrams [[83](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400929607_Can_Vision-Language_Models_See_Squares_Text-Recognition_Mediates_Spatial_Reasoning_Across_Three_Model_Families)]. Therefore, any evaluation of new VLMs for Phase 2 must include rigorous testing on tasks that probe geometric and spatial reasoning, not just general image captioning. Benchmarks like CompareBench, which assesses quantity and spatial comparison, could provide valuable metrics for this evaluation [[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.22737v1)].

Given this analysis, a clear path for upgrading the multimodal components of the HOARD pipeline emerges. For Phase 1, replacing GLM-OCR with a layout-aware VLM like **Florence-2** is strongly recommended. Its specialized design for parsing document structure directly addresses the core challenges of reading muddy, grid-based context sheets. A hybrid workflow, integrating a high-quality OCR engine with Florence-2's reasoning capabilities, could yield the highest accuracy. For Phase 2, **PaliGemma 2** stands out as the most promising open-weight candidate for an upgrade over Qwen3-VL-8B. Its foundation in the advanced Gemma 2 language model suggests it will produce more fluent and terminologically accurate captions for site photographs. However, any replacement must undergo rigorous testing to ensure it does not inherit the common VLM failing of struggling with precise spatial and geometric reasoning. A custom evaluation suite, comprising annotated photos of excavation sections and artefact plans, is essential to validate any new model's suitability for this critical phase [[97](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-acl.1211.pdf)].

## Ollama v0.5+ Orchestration Patterns for Constrained Hardware

Executing the multi-phase HOARD pipeline on consumer-grade hardware with limited VRAM (6–8 GB) necessitates sophisticated orchestration to manage memory and mitigate latency. Ollama, as the chosen local inference server, provides several features and patterns that, when combined with a well-designed automation script, can create an efficient and stable workflow. The primary challenges are managing sequential model loading/unloading, handling initial inference latency ("cold starts"), optimizing quantization, and ensuring system stability under memory pressure.

The default behavior of Ollama is to keep a model loaded in memory for only five minutes after the last request before unloading it to free up VRAM [[62](https://ollama.readthedocs.io/en/faq/)]. In the context of the sequential HOARD pipeline, this default setting is detrimental. Each transition from one phase to the next (e.g., from Phase 1 VLM to Phase 3 SLM) would trigger a cold start, resulting in a noticeable delay as the new model is loaded into VRAM. To counteract this, the `keep_alive` configuration is the primary tool. By setting `keep_alive` to a specific number of minutes (or phases), a model can be kept resident in memory longer. For the HOARD pipeline, a practical strategy would be to set a moderate `keep_alive` duration (e.g., 1-2 minutes) for the initial models. For transitions between major phases, a more dynamic approach is needed. A Python script orchestrating the pipeline could programmatically control this by starting the second model with a `keep_alive` value equal to the expected duration of its own processing time, ensuring a smooth handover.

The issue of sequential warmup latency is a direct consequence of the cold start problem. The first inference call to any given model after it has been loaded into VRAM is always slower than subsequent calls, as it involves initializing the computation graph and moving data. This initial spike in latency can disrupt the user experience. A robust mitigation strategy is to implement a "pre-warming" routine during the pipeline's initialization phase. Before beginning any actual work, the script should send a simple, dummy prompt to each of the models required for the pipeline (e.g., the VLM for Phase 1, the SLM for Phase 3, etc.). This ensures that when the real data arrives, the models are already primed and ready to respond with minimal additional delay [[73](https://dev.to/johalputt/performance-test-ollama-050-vs-vllm-040-local-llm-inference-latency-on-nvidia-rtx-5090-and-1pol)]. While vLLM has shown lower cold-start latency for single requests compared to Ollama in some tests, Ollama's strength lies in its ease of use and integration, making this scripting-level optimization highly effective [[73](https://dev.to/johalputt/performance-test-ollama-050-vs-vllm-040-local-llm-inference-latency-on-nvidia-rtx-5090-and-1pol)].

Quantization is the cornerstone of fitting large models onto constrained hardware. Ollama supports several quantization levels, with Q4_K_M, Q5_K_M, and Q8 being common options. These represent 4-bit, 5-bit, and 8-bit integer representations of the model's weights, respectively [[60](https://dev.to/mitchell_cheng/my-learning-notes-choosing-the-right-ai-model-and-hardware-237)]. The trade-offs are clear:
*   **Q4_K_M:** This is the most aggressive quantization, offering the smallest VRAM footprint and highest potential throughput. It is likely sufficient for the initial digitization and drafting phases where absolute precision is less critical than speed.
*   **Q5_K_M:** This represents a middle ground, providing a modest increase in accuracy over Q4 at a minimal increase in VRAM usage. Given that archaeological prose demands precision, Q5_K_M should be considered the default standard for all phases, especially for synthesis and compliance.
*   **Q8:** This is the least aggressive quantization, preserving the most model detail and offering the highest potential accuracy. However, it consumes the most VRAM. It should be reserved for the most critical steps, such as the final compliance refinement (Phase 4), and used only if the Q5_K_M version of the model fails to meet quality thresholds.

A resilient orchestration script should implement a tiered quantization strategy. Upon startup, it should attempt to load each model with the preferred quantization (e.g., Q5_K_M). If a model fails to load due to insufficient VRAM, the script should gracefully fall back to the next level (e.g., Q4_K_M). This allows the pipeline to adapt to real-time memory pressure. Memory pressure monitoring can be achieved using GPU profiling commands, which can provide insight into VRAM utilization during model loading and inference [[34](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ritik-mishra-0ba500188_in-part-1-i-covered-the-base-level-commands-activity-7445699664278081536-WFeK)]. If a model consistently fails to load even at the lowest quantization level, the script should log an error and halt the process, preventing the pipeline from producing unreliable results. Newer Ollama versions (v0.5+) may introduce features that simplify this process, such as more granular memory controls or programmatic APIs for managing model lifecycles, which should be explored during implementation [[26](https://dev.to/jamesli/-from-0-to-mvp-in-2-weeks-building-a-production-grade-ai-customer-service-system-322n)].

In summary, the best practice for orchestrating the HOARD pipeline on 6-8 GB VRAM hardware involves a combination of automated scripting and careful configuration. The recommended approach is to develop a Python-based controller that:
1.  Performs a full pre-warm of all required models upon launch.
2.  Uses `keep_alive` settings strategically to minimize cold starts between phases, possibly adjusting the value dynamically.
3.  Employs a tiered quantization strategy, attempting to load each model at the highest feasible precision (Q5_K_M) and falling back to lower precisions (Q4_K_M) only if necessary.
4.  Monitors system resources and implements graceful degradation, logging failures and adapting its strategy to maintain stability and reliability.

This disciplined approach transforms the inherent limitations of consumer hardware into a manageable engineering challenge, enabling a powerful, multi-model AI pipeline to run effectively on a single, accessible workstation.

## Comparative Benchmarking: Cloud APIs vs. Local Models

The decision to maintain a fully local pipeline like HOARD versus leveraging cloud-based Large Language Model (LLM) APIs is a central strategic question for any organization in the heritage sector. This choice involves a complex trade-off between the perceived quality and convenience of cloud services and the foundational advantages of local deployment: privacy, offline capability, and predictable long-term costs. This section provides a comparative analysis of leading cloud APIs—including GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Gemini 2.0 Flash—against the proposed local Ollama models, evaluating them across three critical dimensions: archaeological prose quality, regulatory compliance, and cost-effectiveness under both small-team and institutional usage scenarios.

Cloud APIs present a compelling case due to their state-of-the-art performance. Models like GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and DeepSeek-V3 consistently demonstrate superior reasoning, fluency, and command of specialized terminology in evaluations [[22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-10097-9), [37](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/new-leader-open-source-ai-deepseek-v3-benchmarking-1sryc), [96](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10664-026-10858-8)]. They generally produce more polished and coherent prose than even the best open-source models, which is a significant advantage when generating professional grey literature reports [[23](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X26000275)]. Furthermore, these platforms offer vast context windows, with models like Gemini 1.5 Pro supporting up to 1 million tokens, far exceeding the typical capacities of open-source counterparts and enabling a more holistic analysis of entire excavation reports in a single pass [[93](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/vivek-panday_tokenoptimization-aiengineering-llm-activity-7430859389160558592-pr2V)]. The operational simplicity is another major draw; users get near-instantaneous responses without any local hardware setup or maintenance overhead [[73](https://dev.to/johalputt/performance-test-ollama-050-vs-vllm-040-local-llm-inference-latency-on-nvidia-rtx-5090-and-1pol)]. However, these benefits come with substantial drawbacks. The most significant is cost. Pricing is typically charged per token, meaning a single report with 20-50 context sheets (~30K input tokens and ~5K output tokens) would incur a non-trivial expense [[61](https://openai.com/api/pricing/)]. At an institutional scale of hundreds of reports per month, these per-token costs accumulate into a significant and unpredictable operational expenditure [[25](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justindnorman_localllm-openclaw-ollama-activity-7434418478742265856-fxlW)]. More critically, reliance on a third-party API introduces major privacy and security risks. Transmitting sensitive field notes, survey data, and draft reports over the internet is unacceptable for many heritage organizations bound by strict data governance policies. Finally, there is an inherent risk regarding regulatory compliance. Using a black-box model for generating legally defensible documents raises questions about accountability and verifiability, especially concerning adherence to specific regulations like those from the Council for British Archaeology (CIfA) or Section 106 of the UK Town and Country Planning Act [[77](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.13811)].

In contrast, the local HOARD pipeline offers a fundamentally different value proposition. Its defining strengths are privacy and offline operation. All data, from raw field notes to final reports, remains on-site, completely insulated from external networks. This eliminates privacy concerns and makes the system viable for fieldwork in remote locations without internet connectivity. From a financial perspective, the model is overwhelmingly favorable at scale. After the initial capital investment in hardware, the marginal cost per report is effectively zero, creating a highly predictable and low-cost operational model [[25](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justindnorman_localllm-openclaw-ollama-activity-7434418478742265856-fxlW)]. This economic advantage becomes decisive for institutional users who produce a high volume of reports. While local models may not yet match the prose quality of top-tier cloud APIs, the gap is closing rapidly. Architecturally refined SLMs and models trained with a focus on data quality are achieving impressive results on technical and structured text [[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08905v1), [71](https://aclanthology.org/2025.tsar-1.22.pdf)]. Moreover, the local nature of the pipeline allows for a key advantage: customization. The models can be fine-tuned on proprietary datasets of compliant reports, teaching the model the specific formatting, terminology, and reasoning patterns required by different jurisdictions [[9](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sebastianraschka_i-shared-a-new-tutorial-experiments-on-activity-7314290026639462400-NpEJ), [10](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3774628)]. This process of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) can further enhance performance by grounding the model's responses in verified, domain-specific knowledge [[85](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.18910v1), [87](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396290953_A_Comprehensive_Survey_of_Retrieval-Augmented_Generation_RAG_Evaluation_and_Benchmarks_Perspectives_from_Information_Retrieval_and_LLM)].

The following decision matrix outlines the comparative advantages of each approach across different scenarios.

| Factor / Scenario | Local (HOARD Pipeline) | Cloud (APIs like GPT-4o/Claude) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Sensitivity** | **✅ Excellent:** Full data control and confidentiality. | **❌ Unsuitable:** Data is transmitted externally. |
| **Offline Capability** | **✅ Excellent:** No internet connection required. | **❌ Not Possible:** Requires constant, reliable connectivity. |
| **Prose Quality** | **🟡 Good to Very Good:** Competitive with fine-tuning; may lack nuance. | **✅ Superior:** State-of-the-art reasoning and stylistic fluency. |
| **Regulatory Compliance** | **🟢 Reliable:** Allows auditing, validation, and fine-tuning on compliant examples. | **🔴 Uncertain:** Black box; cannot guarantee adherence to niche regulations. |
| **Small-Team Scale (<100/month)** | **✅ Highly Recommended:** Low marginal cost, high privacy. | **🟡 Acceptable:** Cost is manageable for a few dozen reports. |
| **Institutional Scale (>100/month)** | **✅ Highly Recommended:** Massive cost savings at scale. | **❌ Cost-Prohibitive:** Token costs become substantial. |

This analysis leads to a clear conclusion: the choice between local and cloud is not a simple binary but a situational one. For projects involving sensitive data, remote fieldwork, or institutional budgets, the local HOARD pipeline is not just a viable option but the superior and often mandatory choice. The privacy, offline capability, and long-term cost predictability are powerful differentiators that outweigh the transient quality advantages of cloud APIs in most heritage contexts. The cloud should be viewed not as a replacement for the core local workflow, but as a supplementary tool that could be used for specific, non-sensitive tasks—such as brainstorming report structures or summarizing public-domain background research—if an organization's policies permit. The strategic imperative for the HOARD pipeline is to continue evolving, leveraging the latest open-weight models to close the remaining performance gap while steadfastly upholding its core principles of privacy and self-sufficiency.

## Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations for the HOARD Pipeline

The comprehensive analysis of the AI model landscape as of June 2026 confirms that the HOARD pipeline's core strategy of local, private, and customizable AI-driven report generation remains not only valid but strategically sound. The rapid advancements in open-weight models have not rendered this approach obsolete; rather, they provide a rich set of tools to enhance its performance and expand its capabilities. The optimal path forward is not to abandon the local-first philosophy, but to evolve the pipeline by selectively upgrading its components with newer, more capable models where the gains justify the effort, all while adhering to the critical constraints of hardware and domain specificity. A wholesale replacement of all models is neither necessary nor advisable; a pragmatic, phased-upgrade strategy is the most effective approach.

Based on the findings, the following specific recommendations are made for updating the HOARD pipeline:

**Recommendation for Phase 1 (Digitisation):** Replace the incumbent GLM-OCR model with **Florence-2**. The primary reason for this change is Florence-2's explicit design for layout-aware document understanding, a critical capability for accurately interpreting the complex, grid-based structure of archaeological context sheets [[69](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/357124534_LayoutReader_Pre-training_of_Text_and_Layout_for_Reading_Order_Detection), [80](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/WACV2024/papers/Feinglass_Towards_Addressing_the_Misalignment_of_Object_Proposal_Evaluation_for_Vision-Language_WACV_2024_paper.pdf)]. Its ability to parse reading order and identify semantic components within a document directly addresses the core challenges of poor handwriting and messy backgrounds more effectively than a pure OCR engine [[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.08746v1)]. Implementing a hybrid workflow that pairs Florence-2 with a pre-processing stage for image enhancement and a high-fidelity OCR engine would maximize accuracy [[4](https://openreview.net/pdf/e9eaf3d533ddb4c4edd16142a51fbe39cb9244a7.pdf), [31](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/9480013/image-processing-to-improve-tesseract-ocr-accuracy)].

**Recommendation for Phase 2 (Spatial Reconstruction):** Upgrade from Qwen3-VL-8B to **PaliGemma 2**. This recommendation is driven by PaliGemma 2's foundation on the advanced Gemma 2 language model, which is expected to produce more fluent, detailed, and terminologically accurate captions for site photographs [[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.03555v1)]. This improvement is vital for generating high-quality descriptions of stratigraphy and artefacts. However, this upgrade must be accompanied by rigorous testing to ensure the model's spatial reasoning capabilities are robust, as many VLMs still struggle with fundamental geometric understanding [[83](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400929607_Can_Vision-Language_Models_See_Squares_Text-Recognition_Mediates_Spatial_Reasoning_Across_Three_Model_Families)].

**Recommendation for Phase 3 (Synthesis & Drafting):** Retain the current **Qwen3.5-4B** model as a reliable baseline. However, conduct a direct comparative evaluation against **DeepSeek-V3**, a leading open-weight model with a permissive Apache 2.0 license [[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2306.02781v4), [37](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/new-leader-open-source-ai-deepseek-v3-benchmarking-1sryc)]. If DeepSeek-V3 meets the VRAM and context window requirements, it should be adopted for Phase 3. Its reported superior reasoning capabilities suggest it could produce more coherent and insightful synthesized reports [[96](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10664-026-10858-8)].

**Recommendation for Phase 4 (Compliance Refinement):** Maintain the use of **Gemma 4-E2B**. Its small size and proven utility in institutional settings for tasks requiring precision make it an excellent fit for jurisdiction-specific restructuring [[77](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.13811)]. The primary effort should be placed on fine-tuning this model with a corpus of compliant grey literature to tailor its output to specific regulatory standards like CIfA guidelines or Section 106 requirements [[7](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/22/4507), [77](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.13811)].

**Recommendation for Orchestration:** Implement a Python-based orchestration script to manage the pipeline on Ollama v0.5+. This script must incorporate a pre-warming routine to eliminate cold-start latency, use dynamic `keep_alive` settings to smoothen transitions between phases, and employ a tiered quantization strategy (defaulting to Q5_K_M and falling back to Q4_K_M if necessary) to balance quality and VRAM usage [[60](https://dev.to/mitchell_cheng/my-learning-notes-choosing-the-right-ai-model-and-hardware-237), [62](https://ollama.readthedocs.io/en/faq/)]. This systematic approach is essential for maximizing the performance of the pipeline on the target 6GB VRAM hardware.

In synthesizing these technical upgrades, the overarching strategic recommendation is to **maintain and invest in the HOARD pipeline**. The unique combination of privacy, offline functionality, and long-term cost-effectiveness provides an indispensable service to the archaeological community, particularly for institutions with strict data governance policies or those working in resource-limited environments. While cloud APIs offer impressive quality, they cannot replicate the foundational trust and control afforded by a local system. The future of the HOARD pipeline lies in continued incremental improvement, leveraging the burgeoning open-weight ecosystem to refine its performance while staying true to its core mission of empowering archaeologists with secure and efficient AI tools.
